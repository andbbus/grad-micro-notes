32 5.2 Nash Equilibrium

MWG Reference: Ch. 8, §8.D, pp. 246–260

32.1 Context

Nash equilibrium (Nash 1950) is the central solution concept of non-cooperative game theory. It requires that each player’s strategy is a best response to the strategies of all other players — a condition of mutual best response. This is the natural stability condition: in a Nash equilibrium, no player has a unilateral incentive to deviate. The deep mathematical question is whether such a fixed point of the best-response correspondence always exists. Nash’s answer — yes, in finite games if we allow mixed strategies — follows from Kakutani’s fixed point theorem and is the primary existence result in all of game theory.

32.2 Definitions

NoteDefinition 5.2.1 — Best Response Correspondence

Given a normal form game \(\Gamma = (I, (S_i), (u_i))\), the best response correspondence of player \(i\) is \[B_i(s_{-i}) = \arg\max_{s_i \in S_i} u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) = \{s_i \in S_i : u_i(s_i, s_{-i}) \geq u_i(s_i', s_{-i})\; \forall s_i' \in S_i\}.\] This is a correspondence \(B_i: S_{-i} \rightrightarrows S_i\); it may be multi-valued.

NoteDefinition 5.2.2 — Nash Equilibrium (Nash 1950)

A strategy profile \(s^* = (s_1^*, \ldots, s_n^*) \in S\) is a Nash equilibrium (NE) if, for every player \(i \in I\): \[s_i^* \in B_i(s_{-i}^*),\] or equivalently, \[u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*) \quad \text{for all } s_i \in S_i \text{ and all } i \in I.\] Each player is best-responding to the equilibrium strategies of all other players.

CautionRemark

A Nash equilibrium is a fixed point of the joint best-response correspondence \(B: S \rightrightarrows S\) defined by \(B(s) = \prod_{i \in I} B_i(s_{-i})\). The existence problem is precisely: does \(B\) have a fixed point?

NoteDefinition 5.2.3 — Mixed Extension

The mixed extension of \(\Gamma\) is the game \(\hat{\Gamma} = (I, (\Delta(S_i)), (\hat{u}_i))\) where \(\Delta(S_i)\) is the set of probability distributions over \(S_i\) and \[\hat{u}_i(\sigma) = \sum_{s \in S} \left(\prod_{j \in I} \sigma_j(s_j)\right) u_i(s)\] is the expected payoff under mixed strategy profile \(\sigma = (\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n)\).

32.3 Main Results

ImportantTheorem 5.2.1 — Nash Existence Theorem (Theorem 8.D.3 in MWG)

Every finite normal form game \(\Gamma = (I, (S_i)_{i \in I}, (u_i)_{i \in I})\) has at least one Nash equilibrium in mixed strategies.

TipProof

We apply Kakutani’s fixed point theorem (see 1.4 Fixed Point Theorems) to the joint best-response correspondence of the mixed extension.

Step 1: Define the domain. Let \(\Delta_i = \Delta(S_i)\) be the set of mixed strategies for player \(i\). Since \(S_i\) is finite with \(|S_i| = m_i\), \(\Delta_i\) is the standard \((m_i - 1)\)-simplex in \(\mathbb{R}^{m_i}\). The joint strategy space is \[\Delta = \prod_{i \in I} \Delta_i \subset \mathbb{R}^{\sum_i m_i}.\] \(\Delta\) is nonempty (contains the uniform distribution), compact (closed and bounded in finite-dimensional space), and convex (product of convex sets).

Step 2: Define the best-response correspondence. For each player \(i\), define \[\beta_i(\sigma_{-i}) = \arg\max_{\sigma_i \in \Delta_i} \hat{u}_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}) \subseteq \Delta_i.\] Define the joint correspondence \(\beta: \Delta \rightrightarrows \Delta\) by \(\beta(\sigma) = \prod_{i \in I} \beta_i(\sigma_{-i})\).

Step 3: \(\beta\) has nonempty, convex values. Since \(S_i\) is finite, \(\hat{u}_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i})\) is linear (hence continuous) in \(\sigma_i\), and \(\Delta_i\) is compact. By the Weierstrass theorem, \(\beta_i(\sigma_{-i})\) is nonempty for every \(\sigma_{-i}\). Furthermore, \(\hat{u}_i\) is linear in \(\sigma_i\), so the set of maximizers is a convex subset of \(\Delta_i\) (it is an intersection of a linear function’s preimage with a simplex). Hence \(\beta_i(\sigma_{-i})\) is convex, and so is \(\beta(\sigma) = \prod_i \beta_i(\sigma_{-i})\).

Step 4: \(\beta\) is upper hemicontinuous (UHC). We use the closed graph characterization. Let \(\sigma^n \to \sigma\) in \(\Delta\) and \(\tau^n \to \tau\) with \(\tau^n \in \beta(\sigma^n)\) for all \(n\). We must show \(\tau \in \beta(\sigma)\).

Since \(\tau_i^n \in \beta_i(\sigma_{-i}^n)\), we have \(\hat{u}_i(\tau_i^n, \sigma_{-i}^n) \geq \hat{u}_i(\rho_i, \sigma_{-i}^n)\) for all \(\rho_i \in \Delta_i\).

Taking limits (using continuity of \(\hat{u}_i\), which is a multilinear polynomial, hence continuous): \[\hat{u}_i(\tau_i, \sigma_{-i}) \geq \hat{u}_i(\rho_i, \sigma_{-i}) \quad \forall \rho_i \in \Delta_i.\] Therefore \(\tau_i \in \beta_i(\sigma_{-i})\), and \(\tau \in \beta(\sigma)\). Thus \(\beta\) has a closed graph, which (since \(\Delta\) is compact) is equivalent to upper hemicontinuity.

Step 5: Apply Kakutani. The correspondence \(\beta: \Delta \rightrightarrows \Delta\) satisfies all conditions of Kakutani’s fixed point theorem: - \(\Delta\) is compact, convex, and nonempty - \(\beta\) has nonempty, convex values - \(\beta\) is UHC

Therefore there exists \(\sigma^* \in \Delta\) such that \(\sigma^* \in \beta(\sigma^*)\), i.e., \(\sigma_i^* \in \beta_i(\sigma_{-i}^*)\) for all \(i\). This is precisely a Nash equilibrium in mixed strategies. \(\blacksquare\)

ImportantTheorem 5.2.2 — Dominant Strategy Equilibria and NE

If every player \(i\) has a strictly dominant strategy \(s_i^d\), then \(s^d = (s_1^d, \ldots, s_n^d)\) is the unique Nash equilibrium.

TipProof

First, \(s^d\) is a NE: \(s_i^d\) strictly dominates all \(s_i \neq s_i^d\), so in particular \(u_i(s_i^d, s_{-i}^d) > u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^d)\) for all \(s_i \neq s_i^d\). Therefore \(s_i^d \in B_i(s_{-i}^d)\) for all \(i\), making \(s^d\) a NE.

Second, uniqueness: suppose \(s^*\) is any NE. Then \(s_i^* \in B_i(s_{-i}^*)\), which requires \(u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*)\) for all \(s_i\). But \(s_i^d\) strictly dominates \(s_i^*\) (if \(s_i^* \neq s_i^d\)), yielding \(u_i(s_i^d, s_{-i}^*) > u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*)\), a contradiction. Therefore \(s_i^* = s_i^d\) for all \(i\). \(\blacksquare\)

ImportantTheorem 5.2.3 — NE Survives IESDS

If \(s^*\) is a Nash equilibrium and \(S^\infty\) is the IESDS outcome, then \(s^* \in S^\infty\).

TipProof

We show by induction on the elimination round \(k\) that \(s_i^* \in S_i^k\) for all \(i\) and all \(k\).

Base case (\(k=0\)): \(S^0 = S\), so trivially \(s_i^* \in S_i^0\).

Inductive step: Suppose \(s_i^* \in S_i^{k-1}\) for all \(i\). Suppose for contradiction that \(s_i^*\) is eliminated at round \(k\), i.e., there exists \(s_i' \in S_i^{k-1}\) such that \(u_i(s_i', s_{-i}) > u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i})\) for all \(s_{-i} \in S_{-i}^{k-1}\).

By the inductive hypothesis, \(s_{-i}^* \in S_{-i}^{k-1}\). Therefore \(u_i(s_i', s_{-i}^*) > u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*)\), contradicting \(s_i^* \in B_i(s_{-i}^*)\).

Therefore \(s_i^* \in S_i^k\) for all \(k\), so \(s^* \in S^\infty\). \(\blacksquare\)

32.4 Properties of Nash Equilibrium

Failure of uniqueness. NE need not be unique. Coordination games have two pure NE; Battle of the Sexes has two pure NE plus one mixed NE. Non-uniqueness is the rule, not the exception, in economics applications.

Failure of Pareto optimality. The Prisoner’s Dilemma has a unique NE that is Pareto dominated. This reflects the fundamental insight that individual rationality does not imply collective efficiency. Market failures (externalities, public goods) are essentially NE phenomena where the NE outcome fails the Pareto criterion.

NE as self-enforcing agreement. If players could meet before the game to agree on strategies, the only agreements that would actually be honored (without enforcement) are Nash equilibria: if \(s^*\) is not a NE, some player would prefer to deviate unilaterally.

NE as steady state of learning. If players repeatedly play the same game and adjust strategies toward best responses, any rest point of such adjustment dynamics must be a NE. This provides a dynamic justification for the static concept.

NE as consistent beliefs. A NE can be interpreted as a situation where each player’s belief about others’ play exactly matches actual play. The epistemic conditions for NE are: rationality plus mutual correct beliefs. This is strictly stronger than common knowledge of rationality (which gives only rationalizability).

32.5 Example: Computing All Nash Equilibria

NoteExample 5.2.1 — Complete NE Analysis of a 2×2 Game

Consider the Battle of the Sexes: \[\begin{array}{c|cc} & O & F \\ \hline O & 2,1 & 0,0 \\ F & 0,0 & 1,2 \end{array}\]

Pure strategy NE: - \((O,O)\): Player 1 gets 2, best response to \(O\) is \(O\) (since \(2>0\)). Player 2 gets 1, best response to \(O\) is \(O\) (since \(1>0\)). Both best-responding. NE. - \((F,F)\): Player 1 gets 1, best response to \(F\) is \(F\) (since \(1>0\)). Player 2 gets 2, best response to \(F\) is \(F\) (since \(2>0\)). NE. - \((O,F)\) and \((F,O)\): payoff 0 for both; either player can deviate to get positive payoff. Not NE.

Mixed strategy NE: Let \(\sigma_1 = (p, 1-p)\) and \(\sigma_2 = (q, 1-q)\) where \(p = \Pr_1(O)\) and \(q = \Pr_2(O)\).

For player 1 to mix, they must be indifferent: \[\hat{u}_1(O, \sigma_2) = 2q + 0(1-q) = 2q\] \[\hat{u}_1(F, \sigma_2) = 0 \cdot q + 1(1-q) = 1-q\] Indifference: \(2q = 1-q \Rightarrow q = 1/3\).

For player 2 to mix, they must be indifferent: \[\hat{u}_2(\sigma_1, O) = 1 \cdot p + 0 \cdot (1-p) = p\] \[\hat{u}_2(\sigma_1, F) = 0 \cdot p + 2(1-p) = 2-2p\] Indifference: \(p = 2-2p \Rightarrow p = 2/3\).

Mixed NE: \(\sigma_1^* = (2/3, 1/3)\), \(\sigma_2^* = (1/3, 2/3)\).

Expected payoffs at mixed NE: \(\hat{u}_1 = 2/3\), \(\hat{u}_2 = 2/3\). Both players are worse off than at either pure NE — a characteristic feature of mixed NE in coordination-type games.
