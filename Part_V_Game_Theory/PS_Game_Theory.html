38.1 Problem 1 — Nash Equilibria of a 3×3 Game

Consider the following normal form game where Player 1 chooses rows (\(T, M, B\)) and Player 2 chooses columns (\(L, C, R\)):

\[\begin{array}{c|ccc} & L & C & R \\ \hline T & 4,3 & 0,0 & 3,4 \\ M & 2,2 & 5,5 & 1,1 \\ B & 3,4 & 0,0 & 4,3 \end{array}\]

(a) Apply IESDS. State which strategies are eliminated at each round and justify each elimination.

(b) Find all pure strategy Nash equilibria.

(c) Find all mixed strategy Nash equilibria. (Hint: by part (a), you may work with the reduced game.)

(d) Verify that every pure strategy NE you found in (b) is rationalizable. Show that the IESDS outcome from (a) coincides with the set of rationalizable strategies.

(e) Compute the payoffs of each player at every NE you found. Assess whether any NE Pareto dominates another.

38.2 Problem 2 — IESDS vs. Nash Equilibrium

Consider the three-player normal form game in which each player chooses from \(\{A, B\}\). Player 1 chooses rows, Player 2 chooses columns, Player 3 chooses the matrix. Payoffs \((u_1, u_2, u_3)\):

Player 3 chooses \(X\): \[\begin{array}{c|cc} & A & B \\ \hline A & 1,1,2 & 3,0,1 \\ B & 0,3,1 & 2,2,3 \end{array}\]

Player 3 chooses \(Y\): \[\begin{array}{c|cc} & A & B \\ \hline A & 2,2,1 & 0,1,0 \\ B & 1,0,0 & 3,3,2 \end{array}\]

(a) Perform IESDS and find \(S^\infty\).

(b) Find all Nash equilibria (pure and mixed) of this game.

(c) Explicitly exhibit a rationalizable strategy profile that is not a Nash equilibrium. For the strategy you exhibit, construct the chain of beliefs justifying each player’s choice.

(d) Prove formally that the order of elimination in (a) does not affect the outcome, using Theorem 5.1.1. Exhibit two different elimination orders that produce the same \(S^\infty\).

38.3 Problem 3 — SPE via Backward Induction and Non-Credible Threats

Consider the following 3-stage sequential game:

  • Stage 1: Player 1 chooses \(U\) (up) or \(D\) (down).
  • Stage 2: Player 2, observing Player 1’s choice, chooses \(L\) or \(R\).
  • Stage 3: Player 1, observing both prior choices, chooses \(A\) or \(B\).

Terminal payoffs \((u_1, u_2)\): - \((U, L, A) \to (4, 2)\) - \((U, L, B) \to (1, 5)\) - \((U, R, A) \to (3, 3)\) - \((U, R, B) \to (2, 1)\) - \((D, L, A) \to (5, 1)\) - \((D, L, B) \to (0, 4)\) - \((D, R, A) \to (2, 2)\) - \((D, R, B) \to (3, 3)\)

(a) Draw the extensive form game tree, labeling all nodes and payoffs.

(b) Find the backward induction solution. State the optimal action of Player 1 at Stage 3 for each history, then the optimal action of Player 2 at Stage 2, then the optimal action of Player 1 at Stage 1.

(c) Write down the SPE strategy profile explicitly as a complete contingent plan for each player.

(d) Find all Nash equilibria of the normal form game. Identify which NE are SPE and which rely on non-credible threats. For each non-SPE Nash equilibrium, identify the subgame in which the induced strategies are not a Nash equilibrium, and specify the off-path threat that is non-credible.

(e) Apply the one-deviation principle to verify that your SPE is indeed subgame perfect. Show that no single-information-set deviation is profitable.

38.4 Problem 4 — Critical Discount Factor in Prisoner’s Dilemma

Consider the Prisoner’s Dilemma stage game:

\[\begin{array}{c|cc} & C & D \\ \hline C & 3,3 & 0,5 \\ D & 5,0 & 1,1 \end{array}\]

Players interact in the infinitely repeated game \(G^\infty(\delta)\) with discount factor \(\delta \in (0,1)\). Payoffs are normalized as \((1-\delta)\sum_{t=0}^\infty \delta^t u_i(a^t)\).

(a) Verify that \((D, D)\) is the unique Nash equilibrium of the stage game.

(b) State the grim trigger strategy profile \(\tau\) that attempts to sustain \((C, C)\) in every period.

(c) Compute the critical discount factor \(\delta^*\) such that \(\tau\) is an SPE for all \(\delta \geq \delta^*\). Show all steps of the no-deviation calculation.

(d) Now suppose the punishment is not permanent NE play but rather \(T\) periods of \((D,D)\) followed by a return to cooperation. Derive the critical discount factor \(\delta^*(T)\) as a function of \(T\). Show that \(\delta^*(T) \to \delta^*\) (from part c) as \(T \to \infty\) and \(\delta^*(T) \to 1\) as \(T \to 1\).

(e) Suppose cooperation is \((C,C)\) but deviation is detected only with probability \(p \in (0,1)\) each period (imperfect monitoring). Rederive \(\delta^*(p)\). How does \(\delta^*\) depend on \(p\)? Interpret economically in terms of antitrust enforcement.

38.5 Problem 5 — BNE in Cournot Duopoly with Private Costs

Consider a Cournot duopoly with inverse demand \(P(Q) = 10 - Q\) where \(Q = q_1 + q_2\). Marginal costs are private information: firm \(i\)’s cost \(c_i\) is drawn independently from the uniform distribution on \(\{c_L, c_H\} = \{1, 3\}\) with equal probability \(1/2\).

(a) List all possible type profiles \((c_1, c_2)\) and the complete information Nash equilibrium quantity pairs for each type profile.

(b) A strategy for firm \(i\) in the Bayesian game is a pair \((q_i^L, q_i^H) \in \mathbb{R}_+^2\) where \(q_i^L\) is the quantity if \(c_i = c_L\) and \(q_i^H\) if \(c_i = c_H\). Write out firm \(i\)’s interim expected profit as a function of \((q_i^L, q_i^H)\) given that firm \(j\) plays \((q_j^L, q_j^H)\).

(c) Derive the four first-order conditions for a symmetric BNE. Solve for the symmetric BNE \((q^{L*}, q^{H*})\).

(d) Compare the BNE quantities to the complete information Cournot equilibrium quantities from (a). For which cost types does a firm produce more under incomplete information? Provide the economic intuition.

(e) Compute the ex-ante expected total quantity and expected price under the BNE. Compare to the expected total quantity and price in the complete information equilibria (averaged over type profiles).

38.6 Problem 6 — Correlated Equilibrium in Battle of the Sexes

Consider the Battle of the Sexes:

\[\begin{array}{c|cc} & O & F \\ \hline O & 3,1 & 0,0 \\ F & 0,0 & 1,3 \end{array}\]

(a) Find all pure strategy Nash equilibria. Find the mixed strategy Nash equilibrium and compute expected payoffs.

(b) Consider the correlated distribution \(\mu\) defined by: \[\mu(O,O) = \frac{1}{2}, \quad \mu(F,F) = \frac{1}{2}, \quad \mu(O,F) = \mu(F,O) = 0.\] Verify all the correlated equilibrium conditions. What are the expected payoffs?

(c) Consider the more general correlated distribution: \[\mu(O,O) = p, \quad \mu(F,F) = q, \quad \mu(O,F) = r, \quad \mu(F,O) = s,\] where \(p + q + r + s = 1\) and \(p,q,r,s \geq 0\). Derive the four obedience constraints (one for each player and each possible recommendation). Characterize the set of all correlated equilibria as a convex polytope.

(d) Find the correlated equilibrium \(\mu^*\) that maximizes the sum of expected payoffs \(E_\mu[u_1 + u_2]\). Show that \(\mu^*\) strictly Pareto dominates the mixed NE. Explain why a mediator who can recommend (but not enforce) actions can improve on the Nash outcome.

(e) Show that no Nash equilibrium (pure or mixed) achieves the welfare-maximizing CE payoffs from (d). This illustrates that CE strictly expands the set of implementable payoff vectors beyond NE.

38.7 Problem 7 — Folk Theorem: Explicit Construction

Consider the following two-player stage game:

\[\begin{array}{c|cc} & C & D \\ \hline C & 4,4 & 0,6 \\ D & 6,0 & 1,1 \end{array}\]

(a) Find all Nash equilibria of the stage game. Compute the minmax payoff \(\underline{v}_i\) for each player \(i\).

(b) Characterize the feasible set \(V = \text{co}\{v \in \mathbb{R}^2 : v = u(a), a \in A\}\). Sketch \(V\) in payoff space. Identify the feasible and strictly individually rational set \(V^*\).

(c) Consider the target payoff vector \(v^* = (3, 3)\). This payoff requires correlation (verify this). Assume a mediator can publicly correlate players’ actions. Propose a correlated action profile achieving \(v^* = (3,3)\).

(d) Explicitly construct the grim trigger strategy profile \((\tau_1, \tau_2)\) sustaining \(v^* = (3,3)\) as an SPE payoff. Compute the critical discount factor \(\delta^*\) such that no deviation is profitable for \(\delta \geq \delta^*\).

(e) Now consider the target payoff \(v^{**} = (2, 5)\), which is feasible and strictly individually rational. Explain why the simple grim trigger strategy is not sufficient to sustain \(v^{**}\) (asymmetric payoffs require asymmetric strategies). Propose an explicit strategy profile sustaining \(v^{**}\) as an SPE payoff. Compute the critical discount factor.

(f) Verify the full dimensionality condition for this game. Explain intuitively why this condition is necessary for the Fudenberg-Maskin version of the Folk Theorem but not for Friedman’s version.
