29 Problem Set: Choice Under Uncertainty

MWG Reference: Ch. 6; see notes 4.1 State Space Framework, 4.2 Expected Utility Theory, 4.3 Risk Aversion, 4.4 Stochastic Dominance

29.1 Problem 1 — Verifying the VNM Axioms

Let \(C = \{c_1, c_2, c_3\}\) with \(c_1 < c_2 < c_3\) (monetary outcomes: \(c_1 = 0\), \(c_2 = 10\), \(c_3 = 100\)). An agent reports the following preference ordering over a selection of lotteries:

  • \(\delta_{c_3} \succ \delta_{c_2} \succ \delta_{c_1}\) (strict preference over pure outcomes)
  • \(\delta_{c_2} \sim 0.4 \delta_{c_3} + 0.6 \delta_{c_1}\) (indifferent between certain \(c_2\) and the stated lottery)
  • \((0.5 \delta_{c_3} + 0.5 \delta_{c_1}) \succ \delta_{c_2}\) (strictly prefers the 50-50 gamble to certain \(c_2\))

(a) Show that the first two statements are consistent with an EU representation with some Bernoulli utility \(u\). Derive the value \(u(c_2)\) (normalizing \(u(c_1) = 0\) and \(u(c_3) = 1\)).

(b) Show that the third statement (\((0.5\delta_{c_3}+0.5\delta_{c_1}) \succ \delta_{c_2}\)) is inconsistent with the Bernoulli utility derived in part (a) and with EU theory. Identify which axiom is violated.

(c) More carefully: are the first and third statements consistent with the rationality axiom (completeness and transitivity) alone, ignoring the Independence Axiom? Is there any EU utility consistent with all three statements?

(d) Now suppose instead the agent reports \(\delta_{c_2} \sim 0.4\delta_{c_3} + 0.6\delta_{c_1}\) and \((0.5\delta_{c_3} + 0.5\delta_{c_1}) \sim \delta_{c_2}\). Is this consistent with EU? If so, what constraint does it place on \(u(c_2)\)? Explain.

(e) Suppose a fourth outcome \(c_4\) is introduced with \(c_3 < c_4\). Using continuity, argue that there must exist a \(\beta \in (0,1)\) such that \(\delta_{c_3} \sim \beta \delta_{c_4} + (1-\beta)\delta_{c_1}\). What is the interpretation of \(\beta\) in terms of the EU representation? Compute \(u(c_3)\) (normalized so that \(u(c_1) = 0\), \(u(c_4) = 1\)) in terms of \(\beta\).

29.2 Problem 2 — Certainty Equivalent and Risk Premium under CARA

An agent has CARA utility \(u(c) = -e^{-\gamma c}\) with \(\gamma > 0\). The agent faces a lottery \(\tilde{c}\) with distribution \(\tilde{c} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) (normally distributed with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\)).

(a) Compute the expected utility \(EU = \mathbb{E}[u(\tilde{c})] = \mathbb{E}[-e^{-\gamma \tilde{c}}]\) in closed form. (Hint: use the moment generating function of the normal distribution: \(\mathbb{E}[e^{t\tilde{c}}] = e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2}\).)

(b) Derive the certainty equivalent \(CE(\tilde{c})\) defined by \(u(CE) = EU\). Show that: \[CE = \mu - \frac{\gamma \sigma^2}{2}\] Interpret this formula: how does the certainty equivalent depend on the mean, variance, and risk aversion parameter?

(c) Compute the risk premium \(\pi = \mu - CE\) and verify that it equals \(\frac{\gamma\sigma^2}{2}\). Confirm that this is consistent with the Arrow-Pratt approximation \(\pi \approx \frac{1}{2}r_A(c)\sigma^2\) (where \(r_A = \gamma\) for CARA). In this case, the approximation is exact — explain why.

(d) Now consider two agents with CARA parameters \(\gamma_A > \gamma_B > 0\). Both face the lottery \(\tilde{c} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\). Show that agent \(A\) has a lower certainty equivalent than agent \(B\): \(CE_A < CE_B\). Is this consistent with the ordering of Arrow-Pratt measures?

(e) Suppose the agent can purchase insurance that replaces the risky lottery \(\tilde{c}\) with the certain outcome \(\mu\) (full insurance at actuarially fair price). What is the maximum premium \(\pi^*\) the agent would pay for this insurance, as a function of \(\gamma\), \(\mu\), and \(\sigma^2\)? Compare the maximum premium for agents \(A\) and \(B\).

(f) Now suppose \(\tilde{c}\) follows an exponential distribution with mean \(1/\lambda\) (so \(f(c) = \lambda e^{-\lambda c}\) for \(c \geq 0\)). Compute \(CE\) and \(\pi\) for the CARA agent. Is the Arrow-Pratt approximation exact in this case? Under what conditions is the approximation exact?

29.3 Problem 3 — DARA and Risky Asset Demand

An agent has wealth \(w > 0\) and can invest \(a \geq 0\) dollars in a risky asset with net return \(\tilde{R}\) (mean \(\mu_R > r\), variance \(\sigma_R^2\)) and the remainder at safe rate \(r \geq 0\). Final wealth is: \[\tilde{W}(a) = (w - a)(1 + r) + a(1 + \tilde{R}) = w(1+r) + a(\tilde{R} - r)\]

(a) Write down the portfolio optimization problem. Derive the first-order condition for an interior optimum \(a^* > 0\): \[\mathbb{E}\left[u'(\tilde{W}(a^*)) \cdot (\tilde{R} - r)\right] = 0\] Verify the second-order condition under risk aversion.

(b) Use the implicit function theorem to show that the optimal risky investment \(a^*(w)\) satisfies: \[\frac{da^*}{dw} = -\frac{\mathbb{E}\left[u''(\tilde{W}^*)(\tilde{R}-r)\right](1+r)}{\mathbb{E}\left[u''(\tilde{W}^*)(\tilde{R}-r)^2\right]}\]

(c) Show that the denominator satisfies \(\mathbb{E}[u''(\tilde{W}^*)(\tilde{R}-r)^2] < 0\) (under strict concavity of \(u\)). Hence the sign of \(da^*/dw\) is determined by the sign of \(\mathbb{E}[u''(\tilde{W}^*)(\tilde{R}-r)]\).

(d) Prove that DARA (\(r_A'(c) < 0\), i.e., \(r_A(c)\) decreasing) implies \(\mathbb{E}[u''(\tilde{W}^*)(\tilde{R}-r)] < 0\), and therefore \(da^*/dw > 0\). (Hint: use the FOC to show \(\mathbb{E}[u'(\tilde{W}^*)(\tilde{R}-r)] = 0\), then write \(u''(\tilde{W}^*)(\tilde{R}-r) = -r_A(\tilde{W}^*) u'(\tilde{W}^*) (\tilde{R}-r)\), and use DARA and the sign of \((\tilde{R}-r)\) in different states.)

(e) What does the result imply for CARA utility? Verify directly by showing that under CARA, the FOC pins down \(a^*\) independently of \(w\).

(f) Show that under CRRA utility \(u(c) = c^{1-\rho}/(1-\rho)\), the optimal portfolio share \(\theta^* = a^*/w\) (fraction invested in the risky asset) is independent of \(w\) (scale-invariance). Interpret this result in terms of decreasing absolute risk aversion and constant relative risk aversion.

29.4 Problem 4 — Mean-Preserving Spreads and EU Rankings

Consider two distributions over income \(c \geq 0\): - \(F\): uniform distribution on \([2, 4]\) (density \(f(c) = 1/2\) on \([2,4]\)) - \(G\): uniform distribution on \([1, 5]\) (density \(g(c) = 1/4\) on \([1,5]\))

(a) Compute the means \(\mathbb{E}_F[c]\) and \(\mathbb{E}_G[c]\). Compute the variances \(\text{Var}_F(c)\) and \(\text{Var}_G(c)\).

(b) Compute the CDFs \(F(c)\) and \(G(c)\) explicitly. Plot them (or describe their shapes). Do they satisfy the FSD condition \(F(c) \leq G(c)\) for all \(c\)? Explain.

(c) Show that \(G\) is a mean-preserving spread of \(F\) by verifying: (i) Equal means (ii) The SSD integral condition: \(\int_{-\infty}^c [G(t) - F(t)] \, dt \geq 0\) for all \(c\)

Compute \(\int_{-\infty}^c [G(t)-F(t)]\,dt\) explicitly for all relevant ranges of \(c\).

(d) By the equivalence between SSD and MPS, conclude that \(F \succsim_2 G\). Verify this directly for the following specific utility functions: (i) \(u(c) = \ln c\) (log utility, risk-averse) (ii) \(u(c) = c^{1/2}\) (square root utility, risk-averse) (iii) \(u(c) = c^2\) (convex utility, risk-seeking)

For (i) and (ii), confirm \(\mathbb{E}_F[u] \geq \mathbb{E}_G[u]\). For (iii), confirm \(\mathbb{E}_F[u] \leq \mathbb{E}_G[u]\). Compute the expected utilities explicitly.

(e) Construct a new distribution \(H\) that is a mean-preserving spread of \(G\) (and hence also SSD-dominated by \(F\)). Verify your construction by checking the SSD condition.

(f) Suppose a government policy changes the income distribution from \(G\) to \(F\). Characterize the class of social welfare functions \(W = \int u(c) dF(c)\) that rank the new distribution \(F\) as weakly better than \(G\). Can you make a unanimous welfare statement (i.e., a statement valid for all social welfare functions in some large class) about this policy?

29.5 Problem 5 — The Allais Paradox and Violations of Independence

(a) Formally present the Allais paradox as in Example 4.1 of 4.1 State Space Framework. Let \(C = \{0, 1, 5\}\) (millions of dollars). Consider the four lotteries: \[L_1 = (0, 1, 0), \quad L_2 = (0.01, 0.89, 0.10), \quad L_3 = (0.89, 0.11, 0), \quad L_4 = (0.90, 0, 0.10)\] Show algebraically that the preference \(L_1 \succ L_2\) and \(L_4 \succ L_3\) cannot be represented by any EU utility function. (Derive the conditions on \(u(0), u(1), u(5)\) implied by each preference and show they are contradictory.)

(b) Represent the four lotteries as points in the two-dimensional simplex (since \(p_1 + p_2 + p_3 = 1\), use coordinates \((p_1, p_3)\) with \(p_2 = 1-p_1-p_3\)). Draw the lotteries \(L_1, L_2, L_3, L_4\) in this coordinate system. Show geometrically that the Allais preference \(L_1 \succ L_2, L_4 \succ L_3\) corresponds to “fanning out” of indifference curves, which violates the EU requirement of parallel linear indifference curves.

(c) Now consider a rank-dependent utility (RDU) agent with utility \(u(c) = c\) (risk-neutral) and probability weighting function \(w(p) = p^{0.6}\) (a concave probability weighting function that overweights small probabilities). Compute the RDU value: \[RDU(L) = \sum_n [w(F_L(c_n)) - w(F_L(c_{n-1}))] u(c_n)\] where \(F_L(c_n) = \sum_{k \leq n} p_k\) is the CDF of \(L\) evaluated at \(c_n\) (ranked from worst to best). Compute \(RDU(L_1), RDU(L_2), RDU(L_3), RDU(L_4)\). Can RDU with this \(w\) accommodate the Allais preference?

(d) Construct a new pair of lotteries \(M_1, M_2\) and \(M_3, M_4\) with the same underlying structure as the Allais paradox (i.e., \(M_3 = M_1\) mixed with \(\delta_0\) in the same way \(L_3\) is constructed from \(L_1\)) but with different probability values, such that the resulting preferences violate the Independence Axiom. Be explicit about the construction and prove that EU cannot accommodate the stated preferences.

(e) Discuss the policy implications of the Allais paradox. If EU is systematically violated, what does this imply for: (i) The use of expected utility in welfare analysis (cost-benefit analysis of risky policies)? (ii) The design of insurance contracts (if agents have non-EU preferences, does optimal insurance still involve full coverage at actuarially fair prices)? (iii) The use of EU in game-theoretic models of strategic interaction under uncertainty (Bayesian games)?

Provide a balanced assessment: when is the EU assumption likely to be a reasonable approximation, and when is it likely to lead to seriously wrong predictions?
