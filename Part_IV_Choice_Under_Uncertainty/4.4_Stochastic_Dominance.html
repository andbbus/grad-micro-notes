28 4.4 Stochastic Dominance

MWG Reference: Ch. 6, §6.D, pp. 195–211

28.1 Context

Stochastic dominance criteria provide welfare rankings of probability distributions that are robust to the specific utility function, within a broad class. Rather than asking “does agent with utility \(u\) prefer \(F\) to \(G\)?”, stochastic dominance asks “do all agents in some class prefer \(F\) to \(G\)?”. This robustness makes stochastic dominance criteria especially valuable for policy analysis and empirical welfare measurement.

First-order stochastic dominance (FSD) applies to all agents with increasing preferences: \(F\) dominates \(G\) if every agent who prefers more to less prefers \(F\). Second-order stochastic dominance (SSD) applies to all risk-averse agents: \(F\) dominates \(G\) if every concave-utility agent prefers \(F\). SSD is equivalent to \(G\) being a “mean-preserving spread” of \(F\) in the sense of Rothschild and Stiglitz (1970), which formalizes the intuition that \(F\) is “less risky” than \(G\).

These concepts connect directly to empirical welfare analysis: stochastic dominance tests (Barrett-Donald, Linton-Maasoumi-Whang) are used in poverty and inequality measurement to rank income distributions without committing to a specific social welfare function.

28.2 First-Order Stochastic Dominance

NoteDefinition 4.1 — First-Order Stochastic Dominance (FSD)

Distribution \(F\) first-order stochastically dominates \(G\) (written \(F \succsim_1 G\) or \(F \text{ FSD } G\)) if: \[F(c) \leq G(c) \quad \text{for all } c \in \mathbb{R}\] with strict inequality for at least one \(c\).

CautionRemark 4.1 — Interpretation of FSD

\(F(c) \leq G(c)\) means that the probability of being below any threshold \(c\) is smaller under \(F\) than under \(G\). Equivalently, every quantile of \(F\) is at least as large as the corresponding quantile of \(G\): \(F^{-1}(\tau) \geq G^{-1}(\tau)\) for all \(\tau \in [0,1]\). FSD corresponds to the intuitive notion that \(F\) is “uniformly better” than \(G\).

ImportantTheorem 4.1 — FSD Characterization via EU

\(F \succsim_1 G\) if and only if \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\) for all nondecreasing functions \(u: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) (for which the integrals exist).

TipProof

(\(\Rightarrow\)) FSD implies higher EU for all nondecreasing \(u\):

Suppose \(F(c) \leq G(c)\) for all \(c\). Using integration by parts (the key technical tool): \[\int u \, dF - \int u \, dG = \int u \, d(F - G)\]

Integrating by parts on \([-M, M]\) (then taking \(M \to \infty\)): \[\int_{-M}^{M} u(c) \, d(F(c) - G(c)) = \left[u(c)(F(c)-G(c))\right]_{-M}^{M} - \int_{-M}^{M} u'(c)(F(c)-G(c)) \, dc\]

Boundary terms: \((F(M)-G(M)) \to 0\) as \(M \to \infty\) (both CDFs approach 1); \((F(-M)-G(-M)) \to 0\) (both approach 0). Hence: \[\int u \, d(F-G) = -\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} u'(c) [F(c) - G(c)] \, dc\]

Since \(F(c) \leq G(c)\), we have \(F(c) - G(c) \leq 0\) for all \(c\). Since \(u\) is nondecreasing, \(u'(c) \geq 0\) for all \(c\). Therefore: \[-\int u'(c)[F(c)-G(c)] \, dc = \int u'(c)[G(c)-F(c)] \, dc \geq 0\] Hence \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\). \(\checkmark\)

(\(\Leftarrow\)) If \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\) for all nondecreasing \(u\), then \(F \succsim_1 G\):

Suppose for contradiction that \(F(c^*) > G(c^*)\) for some \(c^*\). By right-continuity of CDFs, there exists an interval \((c^*, c^* + \delta)\) on which \(F(c) > G(c)\).

Choose \(u = \mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, c^*]}\) (indicator function, a valid nondecreasing function). Then: \[\int u \, dF = F(c^*) > G(c^*) = \int u \, dG\] — wait, we need \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\), so this is consistent. Let me use the correct contrapositive.

Choose \(u(c) = \mathbf{1}_{[c^*, \infty)}(c)\) (indicator of being above \(c^*\)). This is nondecreasing. Then: \[\int u \, dF = 1 - F(c^*), \quad \int u \, dG = 1 - G(c^*)\] If \(F(c^*) > G(c^*)\), then \(\int u \, dF = 1 - F(c^*) < 1 - G(c^*) = \int u \, dG\). This means there exists a nondecreasing \(u\) for which \(\int u \, dF < \int u \, dG\), contradicting the assumption. Hence \(F(c) \leq G(c)\) for all \(c\). \(\blacksquare\)

NoteExample 4.1 — FSD in Income Distributions

Suppose \(F\) is the income distribution after a policy intervention (e.g., a targeted transfer) and \(G\) is the pre-intervention distribution. If the intervention raises every quantile of the income distribution (every percentile earns at least as much as before), then \(F\) FSD \(G\). All welfare functions that are increasing in income (regardless of inequality aversion) rank the post-intervention distribution as better.

28.3 Second-Order Stochastic Dominance

NoteDefinition 4.2 — Second-Order Stochastic Dominance (SSD)

Distribution \(F\) second-order stochastically dominates \(G\) (written \(F \succsim_2 G\) or \(F \text{ SSD } G\)) if: \[\int_{-\infty}^{c} [G(t) - F(t)] \, dt \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } c \in \mathbb{R}\]

CautionRemark 4.2 — Interpretation of SSD

The condition \(\int_{-\infty}^c [G(t)-F(t)] \, dt \geq 0\) says that the area between the CDFs (with \(G\) on top) is nonnegative up to every cutoff \(c\). Geometrically: the CDF of \(G\) lies “above and to the left” of \(F\) on average (integrated), even if they cross. SSD is weaker than FSD: if \(F\) FSD \(G\), then \(G(t) \geq F(t)\) for all \(t\), so \(G-F \geq 0\) everywhere, and the integral is certainly nonneg. The converse is false — SSD allows crossing CDFs.

ImportantTheorem 4.2 — SSD Characterization via EU

\(F \succsim_2 G\) if and only if \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\) for all nondecreasing and concave functions \(u\) (all risk-averse agents with increasing preferences).

TipProof

(\(\Rightarrow\)) SSD implies higher EU for all nondecreasing, concave \(u\):

Using integration by parts twice. Define \(D_1(c) = G(c) - F(c)\) and \(D_2(c) = \int_{-\infty}^c D_1(t) \, dt = \int_{-\infty}^c [G(t)-F(t)] \, dt \geq 0\) (the SSD condition).

We need to show \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\), i.e., \(\int u \, d(F - G) \geq 0\).

First integration by parts: \[\int u \, d(F-G) = -\int u \, dD_1 = -[u \cdot D_1]_{-\infty}^{\infty} + \int u' D_1 \, dc\]

Boundary terms: \(D_1(\pm\infty) = G(\pm\infty) - F(\pm\infty) = 0\). So: \[\int u \, d(F-G) = \int u'(c) D_1(c) \, dc = \int u'(c)[G(c)-F(c)] \, dc\]

Second integration by parts (integrate \(u'\), differentiate \(D_1\), where \(D_1(c) = dD_2/dc\)): \[\int u'(c) D_1(c) \, dc = \int u'(c) \, dD_2(c)\] \[= [u'(c) D_2(c)]_{-\infty}^{\infty} - \int u''(c) D_2(c) \, dc\]

Boundary terms: \(D_2(-\infty) = 0\) (integral from \(-\infty\) to \(-\infty\)). For \(D_2(\infty) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}[G(t)-F(t)]dt\): if \(F\) and \(G\) have the same mean (\(\mathbb{E}_F[c] = \mathbb{E}_G[c]\)), then \(\int(G-F)dt = 0\) by integration by parts. More generally, for the boundary term to vanish, either \(F\) and \(G\) have equal means, or \(u'(\infty) = 0\) (which holds for concave \(u\) on bounded support or if \(u'\) is bounded and \(D_2\) grows slower than \(1/u'(\infty)\)). Under standard conditions, the boundary term is 0.

Therefore: \[\int u \, d(F-G) = -\int u''(c) D_2(c) \, dc\]

Since \(u\) is concave: \(u''(c) \leq 0\). Since SSD holds: \(D_2(c) \geq 0\). Therefore: \[-\int u''(c) D_2(c) \, dc \geq 0 \qquad \checkmark\]

(\(\Leftarrow\)) If \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\) for all nondecreasing concave \(u\), then SSD:

Suppose for contradiction that \(D_2(c^*) = \int_{-\infty}^{c^*}[G(t)-F(t)]dt < 0\) for some \(c^*\).

By continuity, \(D_2(c) < 0\) on some interval \((c^* - \delta, c^*)\).

Choose \(u(c) = \min(c, c^*) = c \cdot \mathbf{1}_{c \leq c^*} + c^* \cdot \mathbf{1}_{c > c^*}\). This is nondecreasing and concave (\(u' = \mathbf{1}_{c \leq c^*} \geq 0\), \(u'' = -\delta_{c^*} \leq 0\)).

Then: \(\int u \, dF - \int u \, dG = -\int u'' D_2 \, dc = \int \delta_{c^*}(c) D_2(c) \, dc = -D_2(c^*)\)

Wait — using the integration by parts result: \(\int u \, d(F-G) = -\int u'' D_2 \, dc\). With \(u''(c) = -\delta_0(c - c^*)\) (the Dirac mass at \(c^*\), since \(u'\) has a jump down from 1 to 0 at \(c^*\)): \[\int u \, d(F-G) = -\int (-\delta(c-c^*)) D_2(c) \, dc = D_2(c^*) < 0\]

This contradicts \(\int u \, dF \geq \int u \, dG\). Hence \(D_2(c) \geq 0\) for all \(c\). \(\blacksquare\)

28.4 Mean-Preserving Spreads (Rothschild-Stiglitz)

NoteDefinition 4.3 — Mean-Preserving Spread

Distribution \(G\) is a mean-preserving spread (MPS) of \(F\) if: 1. \(\int c \, dF(c) = \int c \, dG(c)\) (equal means) 2. \(G = F + \text{noise}\) in the sense that \(G\) can be obtained from \(F\) by adding noise to each realization: there exists a random variable \(\tilde{\epsilon}\) such that \(c_G = c_F + \tilde{\epsilon}\) with \(\mathbb{E}[\tilde{\epsilon} | c_F] = 0\).

ImportantTheorem 4.3 — SSD Equivalence with Mean-Preserving Spreads

\(F \succsim_2 G\) if and only if \(G\) is a mean-preserving spread of \(F\) (i.e., \(G\) has the same mean as \(F\) but is “more spread out”).

TipProof

Sketch of the equivalence:

(MPS \(\Rightarrow\) SSD): Suppose \(c_G = c_F + \tilde{\epsilon}\) with \(\mathbb{E}[\tilde{\epsilon}|c_F] = 0\). For any nondecreasing concave \(u\): \[\begin{align} \mathbb{E}_G[u(c)] &= \mathbb{E}[u(c_F + \tilde{\epsilon})] \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{c_F}\left[\mathbb{E}[u(c_F + \tilde{\epsilon}) \mid c_F]\right] \\ &\leq \mathbb{E}_{c_F}\left[u\left(\mathbb{E}[c_F + \tilde{\epsilon} \mid c_F]\right)\right] \quad \text{(Jensen, since $u$ is concave)}\\ &= \mathbb{E}_{c_F}[u(c_F)] = \mathbb{E}_F[u(c)] \end{align}\] So \(\mathbb{E}_F[u] \geq \mathbb{E}_G[u]\) for all concave nondecreasing \(u\), i.e., \(F \succsim_2 G\).

(SSD \(\Rightarrow\) MPS): This direction uses the theory of majorization and is more involved. Key idea: if \(F \succsim_2 G\) and they have equal means, then the “excess distribution” \(G - F\) can be decomposed as a series of pairwise spreads (elementary mean-preserving spreads), showing that \(G\) is obtained from \(F\) by adding conditional noise. A full proof uses the Hardy-Littlewood-Polya theorem on majorization.

If \(F \succsim_2 G\) but means differ (\(\mathbb{E}_G[c] < \mathbb{E}_F[c]\)): then \(G\) is worse than \(F\) both due to lower mean (FSD direction) and more spread. SSD captures both effects. \(\blacksquare\)

CautionRemark 4.3 — Rothschild-Stiglitz Interpretation

The MPS characterization of SSD is the key result in Rothschild and Stiglitz (1970). It says: \(G\) is riskier than \(F\) (for risk-averse agents) if and only if \(G\) can be obtained from \(F\) by adding noise. This gives a precise operational definition of “more risky” that does not depend on the particular risk measure used. Applications include: - Insurance: Full insurance eliminates the MPS (replaces \(G = c_F + \tilde{\epsilon}\) with the certain \(c_F\)); this is why all risk-averse agents prefer full insurance at actuarially fair rates. - Portfolio choice: A mean-preserving spread of the return distribution makes the risky asset less attractive to all risk-averse investors. - Principal-agent theory: Performance pay introduces a MPS (adds noise) relative to a fixed wage; risk-averse agents require a premium to accept performance pay.

28.5 Applications

NoteExample 4.2 — FSD and Portfolio Choice

An investor has wealth \(w\) and can allocate it between two assets: a safe asset with gross return 1 and a risky asset with gross return distribution \(\tilde{R}\). A new asset has return distribution \(\tilde{R}' \text{ FSD } \tilde{R}\).

Claim: Any investor with increasing utility (prefers more to less) will weakly prefer the new asset.

Proof: By Theorem 4.1, since \(\tilde{R}'\) FSD \(\tilde{R}\), for any nondecreasing utility \(u\): \(\mathbb{E}[u(\tilde{R}')] \geq \mathbb{E}[u(\tilde{R})]\). Hence any portfolio with the new asset yields higher expected utility for any monotone preferences.

NoteExample 4.3 — SSD and Insurance

An agent has initial wealth \(w\) and faces a loss \(\tilde{L}\) with distribution \(G\) (random wealth \(w - \tilde{L}\)). Full insurance replaces \(w - \tilde{L}\) with the certain amount \(w - \mathbb{E}[\tilde{L}]\) (i.e., distribution \(F = \delta_{w-\mathbb{E}[\tilde{L}]}\)).

The certain wealth \(F\) second-order stochastically dominates the risky wealth \(G\) because: - Both have the same mean: \(\mathbb{E}_F[c] = w - \mathbb{E}[\tilde{L}] = \mathbb{E}_G[c]\). - \(G\) is a mean-preserving spread of \(F\) (adding the zero-mean noise \(-(\tilde{L} - \mathbb{E}[\tilde{L}])\) to the certain outcome).

By SSD, all risk-averse agents (all \(u\) concave) prefer full insurance at actuarially fair premium to bearing the risk.

NoteExample 4.4 — Stochastic Dominance in Welfare Analysis

In poverty analysis, let \(F\) be the income distribution after a social program and \(G\) be the pre-program distribution. If \(F\) SSD \(G\): - All social welfare functions \(W = \int u(c) dF(c)\) with \(u\) concave (inequality-averse) and increasing rank \(F\) weakly higher. - This holds for any poverty measure that can be written as \(P = \int p(c) dF(c)\) with \(p\) concave and decreasing (which includes the Foster-Greer-Thorbecke measures for appropriate parameter ranges).

This is the core result underlying nonparametric welfare dominance tests used in empirical development economics (e.g., comparing welfare pre- and post-policy without specifying the exact social welfare function).

28.6 Empirical Link: Stochastic Dominance Tests

CautionRemark 4.4 — Nonparametric Stochastic Dominance Testing

In empirical applications (poverty and inequality analysis, financial economics), stochastic dominance is tested nonparametrically:

  • Barrett-Donald (2003): Kolmogorov-Smirnov-type test for FSD and SSD between two empirical distributions.
  • Linton-Maasoumi-Whang (2005): Bootstrap-based tests for stochastic dominance that are valid under weak assumptions.
  • Application in poverty: The FSD test asks whether every poverty headcount (at any poverty line) is lower under distribution \(F\) than \(G\). The SSD test asks whether every Foster-Greer-Thorbecke \(P_1\) poverty gap measure (at any poverty line) is lower. These tests can confirm dominance without specifying a poverty line.

This connects directly to the Poverty and Inequality course: the Atkinson (1970) result that SSD is equivalent to unanimity of all increasing concave social welfare functions is the theoretical foundation for these tests.

28.8 Economic Intuition

Stochastic dominance is the natural framework for welfare analysis under uncertainty when the analyst does not want to commit to a specific utility function. FSD is the easy case: if one distribution gives better outcomes at every quantile, all agents with increasing preferences prefer it — this is just a universal improvement. SSD is more subtle: it applies only to risk-averse agents, but it is satisfied in many practical cases (e.g., the comparison of full insurance to a fair lottery, or a more concentrated income distribution to a more dispersed one with the same mean).

The equivalence between SSD and mean-preserving spreads is the deepest result in this note. It says that “riskier” (in the sense of SSD-dominated) is exactly the same as “a mean-preserving spread”: added noise that does not change the mean but makes the distribution more dispersed. This is an operational, non-parametric definition of riskiness that is used throughout insurance theory, financial economics, and welfare analysis.

The policy relevance of these concepts is enormous: stochastic dominance tests allow policymakers to make robust welfare statements about policy interventions without specifying the exact form of the social welfare function. If a policy generates a distribution that SSD-dominates the status quo, then all risk-averse welfare-maximizing policymakers — regardless of their precise inequality aversion — would approve the policy.
