70 Problem Set — Part IX: Model Building for Empirics

Cross-references: All notes in Part IX; 7.4 Signaling; APP Conflict and Resource Models

Instructions: These problems are integrative: they require combining theoretical analysis (formal model solution) with empirical methodology (identification strategy design). For each problem, provide (a) the complete formal model with all definitions; (b) full derivations; and (c) an empirical strategy with explicit identification assumptions and testable predictions. Write all mathematics in LaTeX. Problems are designed at the level of a second-year PhD qualifying examination or an applied theory paper.

70.1 Problem 1 — Extended Spence Signaling Model with Three Types

Background: The two-type Spence model has a unique surviving PBE under the Intuitive Criterion: the least-costly separating equilibrium. Extending to three types creates richer equilibrium structure.

Setup: Three types \(\theta \in \{1, 2, 3\}\) with \(\theta_1 < \theta_2 < \theta_3\). Education cost: \(c(e, \theta) = e^2/(2\theta)\). Wages: \(w = \mathbb{E}[\theta | e]\). Prior: \(\Pr(\theta_i) = \lambda_i\) with \(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = 1\).

(a) Full separation: Characterize conditions on \((\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_3, \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)\) under which there exists a fully separating PBE where \(e_1 < e_2 < e_3\). Derive the conditions on education levels: find the IC constraints for each type (9 constraints), and argue which are binding and which are slack at the optimal separating equilibrium.

(b) Partial pooling: Show that there may also exist equilibria in which \(\theta_2\) and \(\theta_3\) pool at a common education level \(\bar{e}\) while \(\theta_1\) separates at \(e_1 = 0\). Derive the wage for the pooling bundle: \(w(\bar{e}) = (\lambda_2 \theta_2 + \lambda_3 \theta_3)/(\lambda_2 + \lambda_3)\). Write out the IC constraints supporting this equilibrium.

(c) Intuitive Criterion in the three-type model: Apply the Intuitive Criterion to the fully separating equilibrium. Does the least-costly separating equilibrium (where each type’s education is the minimum that deters lower types from mimicking) survive? Show that with three types, the Intuitive Criterion may not fully pin down a unique equilibrium — there may be a set of surviving equilibria. Characterize this set.

(d) D1 criterion: Apply the D1 criterion to the three-type model. Show that D1 achieves a unique selection. State the unique surviving equilibrium.

(e) Testable prediction and identification: State one clear comparative static of the model: how does the minimum separating education level for \(\theta_3\) change when \(\lambda_2\) increases (more middle-type workers)? Design an empirical strategy to test whether the observed education-wage relationship in a labor market reflects signaling. Describe: (i) the ideal natural experiment; (ii) the regression specification; (iii) how you would use variation in cohort composition (\(\lambda\)) to test the signaling model vs. human capital accumulation.

70.2 Problem 2 — Household Agricultural Input Choice Under Price Uncertainty

Background: This problem develops a structural model of household fertilizer demand under output price uncertainty and derives an Euler equation-style moment condition.

Setup: A smallholder household maximizes expected utility over consumption in two periods. In period 1, the household purchases fertilizer \(x\) at price \(w\) and plants a crop. In period 2, the crop yields \(q = f(x, z) \cdot \varepsilon\) where \(f\) is the production function, \(z\) is land quality (exogenous), and \(\varepsilon \sim F\) is an output shock (lognormal with \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon] = 1\)). Output is sold at stochastic price \(p \sim G\), independent of \(\varepsilon\). Period 2 consumption: \(c_2 = p \cdot q - d\), where \(d\) is a debt repayment from period 1 borrowing to purchase \(x\).

(a) Formal model: Write the household’s optimization problem formally. Specify the period 1 budget constraint (borrowing to finance \(x\) at interest rate \(r\)), the period 2 budget constraint, and the objective function \(\max_x \mathbb{E}[u(c_2)]\) where \(u\) is CRRA with parameter \(\rho\).

(b) First-order condition: Derive the FOC for the optimal fertilizer input \(x^*\). Show that it can be written as: \[\mathbb{E}[u'(c_2(x^*)) \cdot p \cdot f_x(x^*, z) \cdot \varepsilon] = (1+r) w \cdot \mathbb{E}[u'(c_2(x^*))]\] Interpret this condition: the expected marginal utility-weighted marginal revenue product of fertilizer equals the cost of capital times the expected marginal utility.

(c) Log-linearization: Under Cobb-Douglas \(f(x,z) = A z^\alpha x^\gamma\) and CRRA \(u\), log-linearize the FOC around the non-stochastic steady state to obtain an expression of the form: \[\ln x^* = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \ln w + \beta_2 \ln p_0 + \beta_3 \ln z + \beta_4 \rho + \text{error}\] Identify each \(\beta_i\) as a function of structural parameters \((\gamma, \alpha, r, \rho)\).

(d) Moment conditions: Write the FOC as a GMM moment condition: \(\mathbb{E}[m(c_2, x^*, p, w, z; \gamma, \alpha, \rho)] = 0\). Specify the function \(m(\cdot)\) explicitly.

(e) Identification: The challenge: output price \(p\) is realized only after the planting decision, but \(p_0\) (the expected price) may be correlated with the household’s productivity \(A\) (good farmers plant in productive areas and also have better information about prices). Propose an instrument \(v\) for \(p_0\) that: (i) is correlated with \(p_0\); (ii) is excluded from the structural equation by the model’s assumptions; (iii) allows identification of the risk aversion parameter \(\rho\). Explain why (ii) holds using the model’s structure.

(f) Welfare calculation: Using the identified structural parameters, compute the certainty equivalent loss from output price risk relative to the risk-free world: \(CE\_loss = \mathbb{E}[c_2(p)] - CE(c_2(p))\) where \(CE\) is the certainty equivalent under CRRA utility. Show how this is a function of structural parameters estimable from the moment conditions.

70.3 Problem 3 — Contest Model with Heterogeneous Groups: Theory and IV

Background: This problem extends the symmetric Garfinkel-Skaperdas contest to heterogeneous groups, derives comparative statics, and designs an IV strategy.

Setup: Two groups \(i \in \{1,2\}\) with heterogeneous fighting costs: group 1 has unit cost \(c_1\) and group 2 has unit cost \(c_2 \neq c_1\). The Tullock contest success function is \(p_i = m_i/(m_1 + m_2)\). The prize is \(R > 0\). Each group maximizes \(\Pi_i = p_i R - c_i m_i\).

(a) Asymmetric Nash equilibrium: Derive the Nash equilibrium \((m_1^*, m_2^*)\) with asymmetric costs. Show that: \[m_i^* = \frac{c_j R}{(c_1 + c_2)^2}\] Verify: \(\sum_i m_i^* = R c_1 c_2 (1/c_1 + 1/c_2)/(c_1+c_2)^2\) … (compute and simplify). At what cost ratio \(c_1/c_2\) is total conflict effort maximized?

(b) Comparative statics: Compute \(\partial m_1^*/\partial R\), \(\partial m_2^*/\partial R\), \(\partial m_1^*/\partial c_1\), and \(\partial m_1^*/\partial c_2\). Interpret each. Show that an increase in \(R\) increases both groups’ efforts. Show that an increase in group 1’s cost decreases group 1’s effort and also changes group 2’s effort — derive the direction of the spillover.

(c) Observable conflict intensity: Let \(I\) denote observable conflict intensity (casualties, incidents) which proxies total effort: \(I = \kappa (m_1^* + m_2^*) + \eta\) where \(\kappa > 0\) and \(\eta\) is measurement error. Derive \(\partial I/\partial R\) in terms of structural parameters. Write this as a regression equation: \[I = \alpha + \beta R + \varepsilon\] where \(\beta\) is a structural parameter. Express \(\beta\) in terms of \((c_1, c_2, \kappa)\).

(d) IV strategy: Suppose \(R = \phi P^w\) where \(P^w\) is the international commodity price (exogenous) and \(\phi\) is a local technology parameter (time-invariant, potentially correlated with unobservables). The regression of interest is: \(I_{kt} = \alpha + \beta R_{kt} + \gamma X_{kt} + \varepsilon_{kt}\). Propose an IV strategy: - State the first stage: \(R_{kt} = \delta_0 + \delta_1 P^w_t \cdot S_k + \delta_2 X_{kt} + u_{kt}\) where \(S_k\) is the resource endowment of region \(k\). Identify \(S_k\) and \(P^w_t\) precisely. - State the exclusion restriction: \(P^w_t\) should be uncorrelated with \(\varepsilon_{kt}\) conditional on controls. State this as a model assumption derived from the Garfinkel-Skaperdas payoff structure. - Discuss threats to the exclusion restriction: what economic channels could cause \(P^w_t\) to directly affect conflict without passing through \(R_{kt}\)?

(e) Heterogeneity predictions: The model predicts that the conflict-resource value relationship is stronger when groups are more equal in fighting capacity (\(c_1 \approx c_2\)). Show this formally: compute \(\frac{\partial^2 (m_1^* + m_2^*)}{\partial R \, \partial \Delta c}\) where \(\Delta c = c_1 - c_2\), and show the sign. Propose an empirical test using cross-sectional variation in group capability.

(f) Dynamic extension: Extend the model to two periods. In period 2, the losing group from period 1 has degraded capacity (a conflict trap). Write down the two-period model and derive whether this creates a complementarity between period 1 and period 2 conflict, generating strategic reasons for early termination or escalation.

70.4 Problem 4 — Technology Adoption with Coordination and Global Games

Background: Technology adoption exhibits strategic complementarities: a technology is more valuable when more others adopt it (network externalities). This creates multiple equilibria. Global games provide a unique selection.

Setup: A continuum of firms, each deciding whether to adopt a new technology (\(a = 1\)) or stick with the old one (\(a = 0\)). The payoff from adoption depends on the fraction \(\phi\) of others who adopt and on a state parameter \(\theta\) (the “technology shock,” e.g., a subsidy or productivity improvement): \[\pi(1, \phi, \theta) = \theta + \rho \phi - k\] \[\pi(0, \phi, \theta) = 0\] where \(k > 0\) is the adoption cost and \(\rho > 0\) is the network externality.

(a) Full information game: Show that with full information about \(\theta\), there are multiple equilibria for intermediate values of \(\theta\): - For \(\theta < k\) (even if all adopt): “No adoption” (\(\phi = 0\)) is the unique equilibrium - For \(\theta > k - \rho\) (even if no one adopts): “Full adoption” (\(\phi = 1\)) is the unique equilibrium - For \(\theta \in [k - \rho, k]\): Both \(\phi = 0\) and \(\phi = 1\) are Nash equilibria Find the conditions precisely and verify.

(b) Global games equilibrium: Now each firm observes a noisy private signal \(x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i\) where \(\varepsilon_i \sim U[-\epsilon, \epsilon]\). Apply the Carlsson-Van Damme argument to derive the unique equilibrium threshold \(\theta^*\) as \(\epsilon \to 0\). Show that \(\theta^* = k - \rho/2\) — the “risk-dominant” threshold at which the firm is indifferent between adopting and not adopting when they believe half of others adopt.

(c) Policy implications: Suppose the government provides a subsidy \(s\), reducing \(k\) to \(k - s\). Compute the new threshold \(\theta^*(s) = k - s - \rho/2\). Show that the probability of the adoption equilibrium (when \(\theta \sim U[\underline\theta, \bar\theta]\)) is: \[\Pr(\text{adoption}) = \Pr(\theta > \theta^*(s)) = \frac{\bar\theta - k + s + \rho/2}{\bar\theta - \underline\theta}\] Compute the marginal effect of the subsidy: \(d\Pr(\text{adoption})/ds\). Interpret as the policy multiplier.

(d) Testable prediction: The global games model generates a regression discontinuity prediction: adoption rates should jump discontinuously at the threshold \(\theta^*\). Propose an empirical design that: - Defines a running variable \(X\) that proxies for \(\theta\) (e.g., a productivity index, a subsidy amount, a demand shock) - Defines the outcome \(Y\) (technology adoption rate in a region or sector) - Identifies the RD bandwidth \(h\) around the threshold - States what a discontinuous jump in \(Y\) at \(X = \theta^*\) would imply about the global games equilibrium selection - Discusses the assumptions needed for the RD to be valid (no manipulation, no other discontinuities)

(e) Multiplicity vs. coordination failure: Distinguish between two interpretations of low adoption rates in the data: (i) \(\theta < \theta^*\) (fundamentals are bad; the unique global games equilibrium is no adoption); (ii) \(\theta > \theta^*\) but coordination failure has selected the bad equilibrium (multiplicity). Describe an empirical test that distinguishes these interpretations. What does the test require theoretically and empirically?

70.5 Problem 5 — Original Model-Building Exercise

Instructions: This problem asks you to build an original formal model, following the methodology of Part IX. Choose a stylized empirical phenomenon from the list below (or propose your own), and complete the full pipeline.

Choose one phenomenon: (A) Firms in conflict zones pay a “war tax” to armed groups in exchange for protection; explain when this arrangement is stable. (B) Mining companies invest more in CSR (community development) in regions with stronger community organizations; explain the mechanism. (C) Humanitarian aid in conflict zones may be captured by armed groups; explain when and how much aid is captured in equilibrium. (D) Journalists self-censor in environments with uncertain reprisals; model when truthful reporting is an equilibrium.

Complete each step:

(a) Define the economic environment \(\mathcal{E}\). Specify: - The agents (who are they? what are their objectives?) - The action space for each agent - The timing (who moves first? what do they observe when moving?) - The information structure (what is private? what is common knowledge?) - The technology (how do actions map to outcomes?)

(b) Derive the equilibrium. Specify the solution concept explicitly. Derive the Nash equilibrium (or PBE, or SPE — as appropriate). Check existence and uniqueness. If there are multiple equilibria, acknowledge this and select using a refinement.

(c) Derive comparative statics. Find the most important comparative static of your model: how does the key outcome change when the key parameter changes? Use the IFT or Topkis as appropriate. Verify the sign.

(d) Derive a testable prediction. Translate the comparative static into a testable empirical prediction. Specify: - The observable outcome variable \(Y\) - The observable predictor \(X\) (the empirical proxy for the key parameter) - The direction of the predicted relationship: \(\mathbb{E}[Y | X = x+1] - \mathbb{E}[Y | X = x] \gtrless 0\)

(e) Design an identification strategy. Propose a credible empirical strategy for testing the prediction: - Specify the regression equation (or matching/RD/DID design) - Specify the instrument \(Z\) if an IV strategy is needed - Justify the exclusion restriction \(Z \perp \varepsilon_Y | X\) using the structure of your model - Describe what data would be needed and where it could be obtained - Identify the main threat to identification and propose a robustness test

(f) Discuss the model’s limitations. List two assumptions of your model that are likely violated in practice. For each, describe: (i) how the violation would change the model’s predictions; (ii) whether the violation would bias the empirical estimate upward or downward.
