62  9.4 — Deriving Testable Predictions

63 9.4 — Deriving Testable Predictions

Cross-references: MWG Ch. 2–3; Chetty (2009); Hausman (1978); Hansen (1982)

63.1 Context

A theoretical model has value beyond pure intellectual interest only if it generates predictions that can, in principle, be confronted with data. A prediction is empirically useful if it restricts the joint distribution of observables in a way that would be violated if the model were wrong. This note develops the methodology of moving from a solved model to empirically testable restrictions: sign restrictions on comparative statics, cross-equation restrictions, moment conditions for GMM estimation, shape restrictions on demand functions, and the sharp testable restrictions generated by classical consumer and producer theory.

63.2 Definitions

NoteDefinition 9.4.1 — Testable Prediction

A testable prediction of model \(\mathcal{M}\) is a restriction on the joint distribution of observable variables \((y_1, \ldots, y_k)\) — i.e., a set \(\mathcal{R}\) such that: (i) if \(\mathcal{M}\) is true, the data distribution satisfies \(\mathcal{R}\); (ii) if \(\mathcal{M}\) is false (or the relevant assumption of \(\mathcal{M}\) fails), the data distribution may violate \(\mathcal{R}\).

A model with no testable predictions is unfalsifiable: no data can distinguish it from its complement. An unfalsifiable model is not science but tautology.

NoteDefinition 9.4.2 — Moment Condition

A moment condition is a population restriction of the form \(\mathbb{E}[m(y, x, \theta_0)] = 0\) where \(y\) are outcome variables, \(x\) are regressors/instruments, \(\theta_0\) are structural parameters, and \(m(\cdot)\) is a known function derived from the model. The GMM estimator solves: \[\hat\theta = \arg\min_\theta \left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_i m(y_i, x_i, \theta)\right)^T W \left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_i m(y_i, x_i, \theta)\right)\] for a weighting matrix \(W\).

63.3 Main Results

63.3.1 Five Types of Empirical Implications

CautionRemark 9.4.1 — Taxonomy of Testable Predictions

Type 1: Sign restrictions on comparative statics. The simplest form. If the model predicts \(\frac{\partial x^*}{\partial \alpha} > 0\), the empirical test is: estimate \(\frac{\partial E[x|z]}{\partial z}\) for an instrument \(z\) that shifts \(\alpha\), and test whether the coefficient is positive. This is essentially a test of the sign of a reduced-form slope.

Example: The contest model (see APP Conflict and Resource Models) predicts that higher resource value \(R\) increases conflict effort. Testable via: does a positive commodity price shock increase conflict incidence? If \(\frac{\partial \text{conflict}}{\partial R} > 0\), and commodity prices instrument for \(R\), a positive IV coefficient confirms the sign restriction.

Type 2: Cross-equation restrictions. Structural models link parameters across multiple equations. Testing these links (overidentifying restrictions) is a test of the model’s internal consistency.

Example: In the permanent income hypothesis, the Euler equation \(\Delta c_{t+1} = r + \varepsilon_{t+1}\) (where \(\varepsilon\) is forecast error) links consumption growth across time periods and requires that consumption growth is unpredictable given lagged information. Cross-equation restriction: the coefficient on lagged income in a regression of consumption growth should be zero.

Type 3: Moment conditions. Structural models often imply that a function of observables and structural parameters has zero expectation at the true parameter value. These are GMM moment conditions.

Type 4: Shape restrictions. Monotonicity, concavity, homogeneity of degree zero — these are non-parametric restrictions on functional forms derived from optimization. Slutsky symmetry is the canonical example.

Type 5: Exclusion restrictions. Variable \(z\) affects outcome \(y\) only through mechanism \(x\) modeled explicitly. The exclusion restriction is the theoretical claim that \(z\) does not enter the structural equation for \(y\) directly — a claim justified by the model’s structure.

63.3.2 FOC-Based Moment Conditions

ImportantProposition 9.4.1 — First-Order Conditions as Moment Conditions

Suppose the agent solves \(\max_{x} u(x, \theta, p)\) subject to \(g(x) \leq 0\) where \(p\) is observed by the econometrician and \(\theta\) is a structural parameter to be estimated. At an interior optimum, the FOC: \[\frac{\partial u(x^*, \theta, p)}{\partial x} = 0\] evaluated at observed \((x^*, p)\) gives the moment condition: \[m(x^*, p, \theta) \equiv \frac{\partial u(x^*, \theta, p)}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x = x^*} = 0\] Under rational expectations, \(\mathbb{E}[m(x^*, p, \theta_0) | p] = 0\) at the true parameter \(\theta_0\).

NoteExample 9.4.1 — Consumer Euler Equation as Moment Condition

Consider the intertemporal consumption problem: \(\max \sum_{t=0}^T \beta^t u(c_t)\) subject to the budget constraint. The Euler equation at an interior solution is: \[u'(c_t) = \beta (1+r_t) u'(c_{t+1})\] Rearranging and taking conditional expectations: \[\mathbb{E}\left[\beta(1+r_t) \frac{u'(c_{t+1})}{u'(c_t)} - 1 \bigg| \mathcal{F}_t\right] = 0\] For CRRA utility \(u(c) = c^{1-\rho}/(1-\rho)\): \[\mathbb{E}\left[\beta(1+r_t) \left(\frac{c_{t+1}}{c_t}\right)^{-\rho} - 1 \bigg| \mathcal{F}_t\right] = 0\] This is a moment condition for \((\beta, \rho)\): for any instrument \(z_t \in \mathcal{F}_t\), \(\mathbb{E}[m_t \cdot z_t] = 0\) where \(m_t = \beta(1+r_t)(c_{t+1}/c_t)^{-\rho} - 1\). GMM uses a vector of such moments to estimate \((\beta, \rho)\).

NoteExample 9.4.2 — Household Agricultural Production Model

A household chooses fertilizer input \(x\) to maximize \(\pi = p \cdot f(x, A, \theta) - w x\) where \(p\) is the output price, \(A\) is land area, \(\theta\) is productivity (potentially unobserved), and \(w\) is the input price. The FOC (at an interior optimum): \[p \cdot f_x(x^*, A, \theta) = w\] Under a Cobb-Douglas production function \(f(x, A, \theta) = \theta A^\alpha x^\gamma\): \[p \cdot \theta \gamma A^\alpha x^{*\gamma-1} = w\] Taking logarithms: \[\ln x^* = \frac{1}{1-\gamma}\left[\ln(p\theta\gamma) + \alpha \ln A - \ln w\right]\] This can be written as a regression equation with structural parameters \((\gamma, \alpha)\) appearing as coefficients on \((\ln p + \ln\theta, \ln A, \ln w)\). The moment condition is: \[\mathbb{E}[\ln x^* - \frac{1}{1-\gamma}(\ln p\theta\gamma + \alpha \ln A - \ln w)] = 0\] The challenge: \(\theta\) is unobserved. An exclusion restriction (input prices \(w\) determined by markets external to the household, i.e., \(w \perp \theta\)) provides the identifying variation. See 9.6 Identification in Structural Models.

63.3.3 Overidentification Tests

NoteDefinition 9.4.3 — Overidentification and the Sargan-Hansen Test

An overidentified model has more moment conditions than parameters: \(\dim(m) > \dim(\theta)\). The \(J\)-test (Sargan-Hansen) tests the joint validity of the moment conditions: \[J = n \cdot \hat{m}(\hat\theta)^T \hat{W} \hat{m}(\hat\theta) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2(\dim(m) - \dim(\theta))\] under the null that all moment conditions hold. Rejection implies either: (a) the model’s structural assumptions are wrong; or (b) the instruments are invalid (correlated with structural errors). The test cannot distinguish between these two interpretations without additional information.

CautionRemark 9.4.2 — Overidentification Test as Model Test

When additional moment conditions come from the model’s structural restrictions (not from additional instruments), the \(J\)-test is a direct test of the model. Failure of the test indicates the model’s structural assumptions are rejected by the data. This is the empirical counterpart of the theoretical question: “Is the model wrong?” Examples: - Testing the Euler equation with different instruments: if the Euler equation is correctly specified, all instruments should be valid - Testing symmetry of demand cross-effects: the Slutsky matrix should be symmetric if consumer theory holds - Testing orthogonality of supply and demand errors: if both equations are correctly specified, the error from one should not predict the residual from the other

63.3.4 Sharp Testable Restrictions from Classical Theory

ImportantProposition 9.4.2 — Slutsky Symmetry as Testable Restriction

From the Slutsky equation, the compensated cross-price effects must be symmetric: \[\frac{\partial x_i^*(p, u)}{\partial p_j} = \frac{\partial x_j^*(p, u)}{\partial p_i}\] Equivalently, the Slutsky matrix \(S(p, u)\) with \(S_{ij} = \partial x_i^h / \partial p_j\) is symmetric and negative semi-definite (NSD). These are testable restrictions on the demand system: run a demand system regression; test whether the cross-price coefficients satisfy the symmetry restriction; test whether the substitution matrix is NSD.

ImportantProposition 9.4.3 — GARP as Testable Restriction (Revealed Preference)

The Generalized Axiom of Revealed Preference (GARP) states: if consumption bundle \(x\) is revealed preferred to \(y\) (directly or indirectly, through a chain of budget-feasible choices), then \(y\) is not strictly preferred to \(x\). Formally: if \(x^t\) is chosen at prices \(p^t\) and \(p^t \cdot x^s \leq p^t \cdot x^t\) (bundle \(s\) is affordable when \(t\) is chosen), then \(x^t\) is revealed preferred to \(x^s\); GARP requires \(p^s \cdot x^t \leq p^s \cdot x^s\) cannot hold (otherwise \(x^t\) should have been chosen at period \(s\), but \(x^s\) is also affordable — contradiction). GARP is testable from panel data on consumption and prices using the Afriat efficiency index.

ImportantProposition 9.4.4 — Factor Demand Symmetry from Producer Theory

For a competitive firm with profit function \(\pi(p, w_1, w_2)\) (output price \(p\), input prices \(w_1, w_2\)): \[\frac{\partial y^*}{\partial p} = \frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial p^2} \geq 0 \quad \text{(supply is non-decreasing in price)}\] \[\frac{\partial x_i^*}{\partial w_j} = \frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial w_i \partial w_j} = \frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial w_j \partial w_i} = \frac{\partial x_j^*}{\partial w_i} \quad \text{(symmetry)}\] \[\frac{\partial x_i^*}{\partial w_i} = \frac{\partial^2 \pi}{\partial w_i^2} \leq 0 \quad \text{(own-price demand non-increasing)}\] All three are testable restrictions on estimated factor demand equations.

63.3.5 The Identification-Prediction Duality

CautionRemark 9.4.3 — Identifying Assumptions Embed Theoretical Claims

A key insight from the model-building perspective: every identifying assumption in an empirical specification embeds a theoretical claim. For example:

  • The exclusion restriction “commodity prices affect household income but not household utility directly” is equivalent to the theoretical claim that households are price-takers in output markets (no market power or subsistence production).
  • The instrument validity condition “conflict shocks are uncorrelated with confounders of the outcome equation” is equivalent to the theoretical claim that conflict is driven by opportunity costs, not by the outcome variable itself.

Making identifying assumptions explicit is therefore a form of model-building: it forces the researcher to state what they are assuming about the economic structure. The best empirical papers present the identification argument as a theoretical claim that can itself be tested (through overidentification tests, pre-trend tests, or heterogeneity predictions).

63.4 Economic Intuition

Testable predictions are the output of the model-building pipeline: they are what the model is for, empirically speaking. The process of deriving testable predictions forces clarity about what the model implies and what it does not. Many models generate a multitude of comparative statics, but not all comparative statics are testable with available data. The art is to identify which predictions are: (a) implied by the core mechanism of interest (not just a feature of a specific functional form); (b) falsifiable with available or feasible-to-collect data; (c) distinct from what competing models predict.

The third criterion is crucial for discriminating between theories. If the model predicts \(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} > 0\) and so does every competing model, the test cannot discriminate. Powerful tests come from predictions that hold uniquely in the proposed model — often from the interaction effects, the heterogeneity of treatment effects, or the cross-equation restrictions that rival models do not share.
