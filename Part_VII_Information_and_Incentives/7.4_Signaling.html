49  7.4 — Signaling

50 7.4 — Signaling

MWG Reference: Ch. 13, §13.C, pp. 450–462; Spence (1973); Cho & Kreps (1987)

50.1 Context

Signaling is the strategic analog of screening in which the informed party moves first. An agent with private information chooses a costly action — a signal — that conveys information to an uninformed observer. Michael Spence’s insight (1973) was that education can be privately valued (it serves as a signal of ability) even if it has no direct productivity effect. The key is that the signal must be differentially costly across types: higher-ability workers find education less costly, so education can serve as a credible signal of ability.

The equilibrium concept is Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (PBE): strategies must be optimal given beliefs, and beliefs are derived from Bayes’ rule on the equilibrium path. A central challenge is that PBE is often non-unique — both separating and pooling equilibria may exist. Equilibrium refinements such as the Intuitive Criterion (Cho-Kreps 1987) and the D1 criterion narrow down the prediction.

50.2 Definitions

NoteDefinition 7.4.1 — Spence Job Market Signaling Model

The model consists of: - Workers: type \(\theta \in \{\theta_L, \theta_H\}\) with \(\theta_H > \theta_L > 0\); prior probability \(\Pr(\theta_H) = \lambda \in (0,1)\) - Education signal: \(e \geq 0\); marginal product (if education is pure signal) is zero; type \(\theta\) pays cost \(c(e, \theta)\) to acquire education \(e\) - Single-crossing: \(c_e(e, \theta) > 0\) (education is costly), \(c_{e\theta}(e, \theta) < 0\) (higher ability → lower marginal cost of education) - Firms: competitive; observe education \(e\); wage = expected marginal product \(w(e) = \mathbb{E}[\theta | e]\) - Payoff: worker of type \(\theta\) with education \(e\) gets \(w(e) - c(e, \theta)\)

The timing is: (1) worker learns type \(\theta\); (2) worker chooses \(e\); (3) firms observe \(e\), form beliefs \(\mu(\theta_H | e)\), set wage \(w(e) = \mu(\theta_H|e) \cdot \theta_H + (1-\mu(\theta_H|e)) \cdot \theta_L\); (4) payoffs realized.

NoteDefinition 7.4.2 — Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (PBE)

A Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium consists of: (1) a strategy for each type \(e^*(\theta)\); (2) beliefs \(\mu(\theta_H | e)\) for firms for all \(e\); (3) a wage function \(w^*(e)\); such that: - Sequential rationality: \(e^*(\theta) \in \arg\max_e \{w^*(e) - c(e,\theta)\}\) for each \(\theta\) - Bayesian updating on path: \(\mu(\theta_H | e^*(\theta)) = \Pr(\theta_H | e^*(\theta))\) via Bayes’ rule wherever \(e^*(\theta)\) is played with positive probability - Free beliefs off path: \(\mu(\theta_H | e)\) can be anything for \(e \notin \{e^*(\theta_L), e^*(\theta_H)\}\) - Competitive wage: \(w^*(e) = \mu(\theta_H|e) \theta_H + (1-\mu(\theta_H|e))\theta_L\)

NoteDefinition 7.4.3 — Separating and Pooling Equilibria
  • Separating equilibrium: \(e^*(\theta_L) \neq e^*(\theta_H)\); each type chooses a distinct signal; firms can perfectly identify types; wages equal true productivity: \(w(e^*(\theta_i)) = \theta_i\)
  • Pooling equilibrium: \(e^*(\theta_L) = e^*(\theta_H) = e^P\); both types choose the same signal; firms cannot distinguish types; wage is the average: \(w(e^P) = \lambda \theta_H + (1-\lambda)\theta_L \equiv \bar{\theta}\)

50.3 Main Results

50.3.1 Separating Equilibria

ImportantProposition 7.4.1 — Characterization of Separating Equilibria

In a separating PBE: 1. The low type chooses \(e^*(\theta_L) = 0\) (no signaling for the low type) 2. The high type chooses \(e^*(\theta_H) \in [\hat{e}, \bar{e}]\) where \(\hat{e}\) is the minimum education level that deters the low type from mimicking and \(\bar{e}\) is the maximum education level the high type is willing to acquire 3. The beliefs support the equilibrium: \(\mu(\theta_H | e) = 1\) for \(e \geq e^*(\theta_H)\) and \(\mu(\theta_H | e) = 0\) for \(e < e^*(\theta_H)\) (or more generally, any beliefs that make deviation unprofitable)

TipProof

Step 1: Low type chooses \(e_L = 0\). In any separating equilibrium, wages perfectly reveal types: \(w(e^*(\theta_L)) = \theta_L\) and \(w(e^*(\theta_H)) = \theta_H\). Since \(c(0, \theta_L) = 0\) (normalize) and the low type earns \(\theta_L\) regardless of signal level (at \(e^*(\theta_L)\)), they minimize signaling costs by setting \(e^*(\theta_L) = 0\). Any \(e^*(\theta_L) > 0\) is dominated by \(e^*(\theta_L) = 0\) since education is costly and does not affect wages (in equilibrium, the low type is identified regardless).

Step 2: Incentive constraints. For separation to be incentive compatible: - Low type should not mimic the high type: \[\theta_L - c(0, \theta_L) \geq \theta_H - c(e^*(\theta_H), \theta_L)\] \[c(e^*(\theta_H), \theta_L) \geq \theta_H - \theta_L \quad \text{(IC-L)}\] - High type should not mimic the low type (i.e., should not deviate to \(e = 0\)): \[\theta_H - c(e^*(\theta_H), \theta_H) \geq \theta_L - c(0, \theta_H) = \theta_L\] \[c(e^*(\theta_H), \theta_H) \leq \theta_H - \theta_L \quad \text{(IC-H)}\]

Step 3: Threshold \(\hat{e}\). The binding IC-L (with equality) defines the threshold: \[c(\hat{e}, \theta_L) = \theta_H - \theta_L\] For \(e^*(\theta_H) \geq \hat{e}\): IC-L is satisfied (the low type does not want to acquire that much education).

Step 4: Continuum of equilibria. IC-H and IC-L together require \(e^*(\theta_H) \in [\hat{e}, \bar{e}]\) where \(\bar{e}\) solves \(c(\bar{e}, \theta_H) = \theta_H - \theta_L\). Any \(e_H \in [\hat{e}, \bar{e}]\), combined with appropriate off-path beliefs, supports a separating PBE.

Verification of beliefs: If the firm believes any education \(e < e^*(\theta_H)\) signals \(\theta_L\) (i.e., \(\mu(\theta_H|e) = 0\) for \(e < e^*(\theta_H)\)), then the equilibrium wage for deviation is \(\theta_L\), and the high type will not deviate as long as IC-H holds.

50.3.2 Pooling Equilibria

ImportantProposition 7.4.2 — Characterization of Pooling Equilibria

A pooling PBE exists at education level \(e^P \geq 0\) with wage \(w(e^P) = \bar{\theta} = \lambda \theta_H + (1-\lambda)\theta_L\) if and only if: 1. Both types prefer the pooling wage at \(e^P\) to the low wage at any deviation \(e' \neq e^P\): \[\bar{\theta} - c(e^P, \theta) \geq \theta_L - c(e', \theta) \quad \forall \theta, \forall e'\] 2. Off-path beliefs assign \(\mu(\theta_H | e') \leq\) some threshold for deviations \(e' \neq e^P\)

TipProof

At pooling education \(e^P\), the wage is \(\bar\theta\). The firm’s off-path beliefs \(\mu(\theta_H | e')\) for \(e' \neq e^P\) can be set arbitrarily, which determines \(w(e')\). A worst-case belief for deviations: \(\mu(\theta_H | e') = 0\) for all \(e' \neq e^P\), so \(w(e') = \theta_L\) for all deviations.

Under these beliefs, neither type wants to deviate if: \[\bar\theta - c(e^P, \theta) \geq \theta_L\] i.e., \(c(e^P, \theta) \leq \bar\theta - \theta_L\) for both \(\theta \in \{\theta_L, \theta_H\}\).

Since \(c_{e\theta} < 0\) (single-crossing), the high type has lower education costs, so the binding constraint is the low type: \(c(e^P, \theta_L) \leq \bar\theta - \theta_L\). This is satisfied for small enough \(e^P\), including \(e^P = 0\).

Therefore a pooling equilibrium at \(e^P = 0\) (no education) always exists with worst-case off-path beliefs.

50.3.3 Equilibrium Refinements

The Intuitive Criterion (Cho-Kreps 1987)

NoteDefinition 7.4.4 — Equilibrium Dominance and the Intuitive Criterion

In a signaling game with equilibrium payoffs \(u^*(\theta)\) for each type \(\theta\): - A message \(e'\) is equilibrium dominated for type \(\theta\) if: \[\max_{w \in [\theta_L, \theta_H]} \{w - c(e', \theta)\} < u^*(\theta)\] i.e., even the best possible wage after sending \(e'\) yields less than the equilibrium payoff.

The Intuitive Criterion requires: if \(e'\) is equilibrium dominated for \(\theta_L\) but NOT for \(\theta_H\) (i.e., \(\theta_H\) could benefit from \(e'\) if firms believe \(\theta_H\) with probability 1), then off-path beliefs must assign \(\mu(\theta_H | e') = 1\).

ImportantTheorem 7.4.3 — Intuitive Criterion Eliminates All But the Least-Costly Separating Equilibrium

The unique separating PBE surviving the Intuitive Criterion has the high type choosing \(e^*(\theta_H) = \hat{e}\) — the least-costly separating equilibrium — where \(\hat{e}\) is defined by \(c(\hat{e}, \theta_L) = \theta_H - \theta_L\).

Moreover, all pooling equilibria are eliminated by the Intuitive Criterion.

TipProof

Eliminating pooling equilibria: Consider a pooling equilibrium at \(e^P\) with equilibrium payoffs \(u^*(\theta) = \bar\theta - c(e^P, \theta)\).

Consider a deviation to \(e' > e^P\) such that: \[\bar\theta - c(e^P, \theta_L) > \theta_H - c(e', \theta_L) \quad \text{(deviation dominated for } \theta_L\text{)}\] \[\theta_H - c(e', \theta_H) > \bar\theta - c(e^P, \theta_H) \quad \text{(deviation not dominated for } \theta_H\text{)}\]

Such an \(e'\) exists because \(c_{e\theta} < 0\): higher types have lower marginal costs, so there is a range \(e'\) where \(e'\) is too costly for \(\theta_L\) but profitable for \(\theta_H\).

Specifically, single-crossing implies that the set of \(e'\) satisfying the second inequality (profitable for \(\theta_H\)) extends above the set satisfying the first (dominated for \(\theta_L\)). Pick any \(e'\) in the gap: it is dominated for \(\theta_L\) but not for \(\theta_H\).

By the Intuitive Criterion, beliefs must put \(\mu(\theta_H | e') = 1\), so \(w(e') = \theta_H\). But then \(\theta_H\) strictly prefers to deviate to \(e'\): \(\theta_H - c(e', \theta_H) > \bar\theta - c(e^P, \theta_H) = u^*(\theta_H)\). Contradiction with \(e^P\) being a pooling equilibrium.

Eliminating non-least-costly separating equilibria: Consider a separating equilibrium with \(e^*(\theta_H) = \tilde{e} > \hat{e}\), so \(u^*(\theta_H) = \theta_H - c(\tilde{e}, \theta_H)\).

Consider a deviation by the high type to \(e' \in (\hat{e}, \tilde{e})\). Since \(c(\hat{e}, \theta_L) = \theta_H - \theta_L\), for \(e' > \hat{e}\): \[c(e', \theta_L) > \theta_H - \theta_L \implies \theta_H - c(e', \theta_L) < \theta_L = u^*(\theta_L)\] So \(e'\) is equilibrium dominated for \(\theta_L\) (even if the firm pays the maximum wage \(\theta_H\), \(\theta_L\) does worse than in equilibrium).

For \(\theta_H\) at \(e' \in (\hat{e}, \tilde{e})\): \[\theta_H - c(e', \theta_H) > \theta_H - c(\tilde{e}, \theta_H) = u^*(\theta_H) \quad \text{(since } c \text{ is increasing in } e\text{)}\] So \(e'\) is NOT dominated for \(\theta_H\).

By the Intuitive Criterion: \(\mu(\theta_H | e') = 1\), so \(w(e') = \theta_H\). But then \(\theta_H\) earns \(\theta_H - c(e', \theta_H) > u^*(\theta_H)\) by deviating to \(e'\). Contradiction.

The only surviving equilibrium is \(e^*(\theta_H) = \hat{e}\): the high type chooses the minimum education that credibly separates.

50.3.4 D1 Criterion

NoteDefinition 7.4.5 — D1 Criterion

The D1 criterion strengthens the Intuitive Criterion. For a message \(e'\) off the equilibrium path, define the set of best responses for the firm that would make type \(\theta\) willing to send \(e'\): \[D_\theta(e') = \{w : w - c(e', \theta) \geq u^*(\theta)\}\] The D1 criterion requires that if \(D_{\theta_L}(e') \subset D_{\theta_H}(e')\) (strictly), then \(\mu(\theta_H | e') = 1\).

D1 implies the Intuitive Criterion. Together with single-crossing, D1 also selects the least-costly separating equilibrium.

50.3.5 Over-Education as Social Waste

ImportantProposition 7.4.4 — Signaling is Socially Wasteful

If education has no direct effect on productivity (pure signaling), then any separating equilibrium is socially inefficient relative to the no-signaling pooling allocation at \(e = 0\). The education expenditures are a social deadweight loss.

TipProof

Under pure signaling, total output is \(\lambda \theta_H + (1-\lambda)\theta_L = \bar\theta\) regardless of the education choices, since education does not affect productivity.

In the separating equilibrium with \(e^*(\theta_H) = \hat{e}\), the total social cost is \(\lambda c(\hat{e}, \theta_H) > 0\).

In the pooling equilibrium at \(e^P = 0\), total social cost of education is \(0\).

Total output in both cases: \(\bar\theta\). Therefore the separating equilibrium has strictly lower total surplus by \(\lambda c(\hat{e}, \theta_H) > 0\).

Intuitively: signaling is a zero-sum redistribution (from firms to high-ability workers) that requires a positive resource cost (education). From society’s perspective, the education expenditures are burned while the allocation of workers to jobs is the same as if there were no signaling. This is a classical result showing that equilibria with private information may be Pareto-dominated by a world without the signal technology.

Caveat: This argument assumes education has zero human capital value. If education raises productivity, the first-best has positive education, and the welfare comparison between signaling equilibria and no-education requires a more careful analysis.

50.4 Economic Intuition

Signaling reverses the informational structure of screening: here, the informed party (the worker) moves first and chooses an action (education) that conveys information to the uninformed party (the firm). The key is that the action must be credible — it must be too costly for low types to mimic.

The single-crossing condition is the engine of all signaling results. Because high-ability workers have lower marginal costs of education, they can credibly signal their ability by acquiring education levels that would be prohibitively expensive for low-ability workers. This creates a separating equilibrium where education levels perfectly sort workers by type, even though education has no direct productivity effect.

The multiplicity of equilibria reflects the self-fulfilling nature of beliefs in signaling games. If firms believe that all workers with \(e \geq e_H\) are high ability, then high-ability workers have an incentive to acquire \(e_H\) — and firms’ beliefs are confirmed. Any \(e_H \in [\hat{e}, \bar{e}]\) can sustain these beliefs. The Intuitive Criterion rules out equilibria with excessive education by noting that firms should be able to “understand” that a deviation to \(e' \in (\hat{e}, e_H)\) must come from a high-ability worker (since the low type would never benefit from it), and therefore should update beliefs upward.
