53 Problem Set — Part VII: Information and Incentives

MWG Reference: Ch. 13–14, 23; Holmström (1979); Spence (1973); Myerson (1981); Kyle (1985)

Instructions: Provide formal proofs for all theoretical results. State assumptions clearly. Use LaTeX for all mathematics. Unless otherwise stated, assume all functions are sufficiently smooth.

53.1 Problem 1 — Optimal Linear Contract in the LEN Model

Consider the LEN (Linear-Exponential-Normal) principal-agent model with the following parameters: - Output: \(q = e + \varepsilon\), \(\varepsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^2)\) with \(\sigma^2 = 4\) - Agent’s CARA utility with risk aversion coefficient \(r = 0.5\) - Effort cost: \(c(e) = \frac{1}{2}e^2\) (so the cost parameter is \(c = 1\)) - Agent’s reservation utility: \(\bar{u} = 0\) (normalizing the outside option) - Contract: linear \(t = \alpha + \beta q\)

(a) Derive the agent’s optimal effort level \(e^*(\beta)\) as a function of the incentive rate \(\beta\).

(b) Using the binding participation constraint, express the base salary \(\alpha^*(\beta)\) as a function of \(\beta\). Show all steps using the certainty-equivalent formula for CARA-normal utility.

(c) Derive the principal’s profit function \(\Pi(\beta)\) after substituting \(e^*(\beta)\) and \(\alpha^*(\beta)\). Take the derivative and find the optimal incentive rate \(\beta^*\). Compute the numerical value using the given parameters.

(d) Compute the equilibrium effort level \(e^* = e^*(\beta^*)\), the base salary \(\alpha^*\), and the principal’s expected profit \(\Pi^*\).

(e) Compare the second-best outcome to the first-best (observable effort). Compute the efficiency loss as a fraction of first-best total surplus. Interpret the result economically.

(f) Now suppose the principal can observe a noisy signal \(s = e + \eta\) where \(\eta \sim N(0, \sigma_s^2 = 1)\) in addition to \(q\). Using Holmström’s Informativeness Principle, determine whether the optimal contract should condition on \(s\). Derive the optimal contract in the augmented LEN model (with both \(q\) and \(s\)), and show analytically that the inclusion of \(s\) Pareto-improves relative to the contract based on \(q\) alone.

53.2 Problem 2 — Two-Type Screening Problem

A monopolist provides quality \(q \geq 0\) to a consumer with private type \(\theta \in \{\theta_L, \theta_H\}\), where \(\theta_H = 3\), \(\theta_L = 1\), and \(\Pr(\theta_H) = \lambda = 1/3\). Consumer utility: \(u(q, t, \theta) = \theta \sqrt{q} - t\). The monopolist’s cost: \(c(q) = q\). Reservation utility \(\bar{u} = 0\).

(a) Compute the first-best allocation \((q_L^{FB}, q_H^{FB})\) and the corresponding first-best transfers \((t_L^{FB}, t_H^{FB})\). Show that the first-best allocations are \(q_i^{FB} = \theta_i^2 / 4\).

(b) Verify that the single-crossing condition holds: show that \(MRS_{q,t}(\theta_H, q) > MRS_{q,t}(\theta_L, q)\) for all \(q > 0\).

(c) Set up the monopolist’s second-best program. Write out all four constraints (IC-H, IC-L, IR-H, IR-L) explicitly. Argue informally which constraints bind at the optimum and which are slack. State the standard argument for why IC-H and IR-L bind while IC-L and IR-H are slack.

(d) Using the binding IC-H and IR-L, express \(t_H\) and \(t_L\) as functions of \(q_H\) and \(q_L\). Substitute into the monopolist’s objective function to obtain an unconstrained program in \((q_H, q_L)\).

(e) Derive the optimal \(q_H^{SB}\) and \(q_L^{SB}\) by taking FOCs. Show that: \(q_H^{SB} = q_H^{FB}\) (no distortion at the top) and \(q_L^{SB} < q_L^{FB}\) (distortion at the bottom). Compute the numerical values.

(f) Compute the information rent earned by the high type: \(U_H = \theta_H \sqrt{q_H^{SB}} - t_H^{SB}\).

(g) Discuss when the monopolist would choose to exclude the low type entirely (shut down the low market). Find the condition on parameters under which exclusion is optimal.

53.3 Problem 3 — Signaling Game: PBE and the Intuitive Criterion

Two types of workers: \(\theta_H = 4\) (high ability) and \(\theta_L = 2\) (low ability), with \(\Pr(\theta_H) = \lambda = 0.4\). Workers choose education \(e \geq 0\). The cost of education is: \[c(e, \theta) = \frac{e^2}{2\theta}\] Wages equal expected ability: \(w = \mathbb{E}[\theta | e]\). Workers maximize \(w - c(e, \theta)\).

(a) Verify the single-crossing condition: \(\frac{\partial^2 c}{\partial e \partial \theta} < 0\) for all \(e > 0\).

(b) Find all separating PBE. Specifically: - Show \(e_L^* = 0\) in any separating equilibrium. - Find the threshold \(\hat{e}\) such that IC-L is satisfied with equality at \(e_H = \hat{e}\). - Characterize all separating equilibria parametrized by \(e_H \geq \hat{e}\). - State the off-path beliefs that support each separating equilibrium.

(c) Find all pooling PBE. Specifically: - Compute the pooling wage \(\bar\theta = \lambda \theta_H + (1-\lambda) \theta_L\). - Find the set of pooling education levels \(e^P\) that can be sustained with off-path beliefs \(\mu(\theta_H | e) = 0\) for \(e \neq e^P\). - State the conditions on \(e^P\) for a pooling PBE to exist.

(d) Apply the Intuitive Criterion: - Show that every pooling equilibrium fails the Intuitive Criterion. Exhibit explicitly the out-of-equilibrium message that the Intuitive Criterion assigns to the high type. - Show that every separating equilibrium with \(e_H > \hat{e}\) fails the Intuitive Criterion. Exhibit the deviation. - Conclude that the unique surviving equilibrium is the least-costly separating equilibrium at \(e_H = \hat{e}\). State the equilibrium explicitly.

(e) Compute the total social welfare loss due to signaling in the least-costly separating equilibrium. Is any party better off compared to the first-best no-signaling allocation?

(f) Testable prediction: State one clear empirical prediction of this model. Describe an identification strategy to test whether the observed education-wage relationship reflects signaling, human capital accumulation, or both. What data variation would allow you to separate these mechanisms?

53.4 Problem 4 — VCG Mechanism in a Public Goods Problem

Three agents \(i \in \{1,2,3\}\) must decide whether to produce a public good (\(k = 1\)) or not (\(k = 0\)). Agent \(i\) has private value \(v_i(k) = \theta_i \cdot k\) where \(\theta_i \in \{0, 1, 2\}\) is their type. The cost of the public good is \(C = 2\), paid from VCG transfers.

(a) Define the socially efficient decision rule \(k^*(\theta)\): the good is produced iff the total value exceeds the cost. Write out the rule explicitly for all \(3^3 = 27\) type profiles (or characterize it generally).

(b) Construct the VCG mechanism for this problem: - Specify the outcome function \(g(\hat\theta)\): allocation \(k^*(\hat\theta)\) plus transfers \(t_i(\hat\theta)\). - Use the Clarke pivot rule: \(t_i(\hat\theta) = C_i(\hat\theta_{-i}) - \sum_{j\neq i} v_j(k^*(\hat\theta), \hat\theta_j)\) where \(C_i(\hat\theta_{-i})\) is the cost borne by \(i\) in the Groves mechanism. Write out the transfers explicitly.

(c) Verify that truth-telling is a dominant strategy for each agent by checking that for each type profile and each possible misreport by agent 1, agent 1’s payoff is weakly higher from truth-telling.

(d) Is the VCG mechanism budget-balanced? Compute the expected net revenue \(\mathbb{E}[\sum_i t_i]\) under the uniform prior over types and show whether it is positive, negative, or zero.

(e) Does the Gibbard-Satterthwaite theorem apply to this problem? Explain why or why not (referring to the quasi-linear domain restriction).

53.5 Problem 5 — Myerson-Satterthwaite: Efficiency Deficit

Buyer has value \(v \sim U[0,1]\); seller has cost \(c \sim U[0,1]\), independent. Trade is efficient iff \(v \geq c\).

(a) Compute the virtual value of the buyer: \(\psi_B(v) = v - (1-F_B(v))/f_B(v)\), and the virtual cost of the seller: \(\phi_S(c) = c + F_S(c)/f_S(c)\). Interpret each.

(b) For the efficient allocation \(q^*(v,c) = \mathbf{1}[v \geq c]\), compute: - \(\mathbb{E}[(\psi_B(v) - \phi_S(c)) \cdot \mathbf{1}[v \geq c]]\)

Show that this expectation is negative, confirming the budget imbalance at the efficient allocation.

(c) The Myerson-Satterthwaite theorem states that budget-balanced BIC-IR efficiency is impossible. Using the formula from part (b) and the IR constraints \(U_B(0) \geq 0\) and \(U_S(1) \geq 0\), show precisely where the contradiction arises.

(d) Find the Myerson-optimal (second-best) bilateral trade mechanism: the mechanism that maximizes ex-ante gains from trade subject to BIC, IR, and budget balance. Show that trade occurs iff \(\psi_B(v) \geq \phi_S(c)\), and find the threshold values of \(v\) and \(c\) where trade occurs.

(e) Compute the efficiency deficit: the probability of efficient trades foregone in the Myerson-optimal mechanism relative to first-best. Interpret this as the cost of private information in bilateral trade.

53.6 Problem 6 — Kyle Model: Informed Trader Profits and Price Discovery

Consider the Kyle (1985) single-period model with: - Asset value: \(v \sim N(0, \sigma_v^2)\) with \(\sigma_v^2 = 9\) - Noise traders: \(u \sim N(0, \sigma_u^2)\) with \(\sigma_u^2 = 4\) - Informed trader submits \(x\); market maker observes \(y = x + u\) and sets \(p = \mathbb{E}[v|y]\)

(a) Derive the linear equilibrium: find the informed trader’s trading intensity \(\beta\) and the market maker’s price impact \(\lambda\). Compute their numerical values.

(b) Compute the informed trader’s expected profit \(\mathbb{E}[\Pi] = \mathbb{E}[(v-p) \cdot x]\). Show that it equals \(\frac{1}{2}\sigma_v \sigma_u\).

(c) Compute the posterior variance of the asset value conditional on the equilibrium price: \(\text{Var}(v | p)\). Show that exactly half the information is revealed in equilibrium.

(d) Now suppose noise trading doubles: \(\sigma_u^2 = 8\). Recompute \(\beta\), \(\lambda\), and \(\mathbb{E}[\Pi]\). How does the informed trader respond to the change? Does their profit increase or decrease? Interpret.

(e) Consider an alternative setup where there are \(n > 1\) strategic informed traders each observing the same \(v\). The informed traders behave à la Cournot (each chooses \(x_i\) taking others’ strategies as given). Derive the symmetric linear equilibrium in this case. Show that as \(n \to \infty\), \(\lambda \to 0\) and prices become fully informative. Reconcile with the Grossman-Stiglitz paradox.

(f) Empirical application: Describe how Kyle’s \(\lambda\) can be estimated from transaction-level data. What regression would you run? What does an increase in estimated \(\lambda\) before a scheduled earnings announcement imply about information asymmetry?

53.7 Problem 7 — Grossman-Stiglitz Equilibrium and Price Informativeness

Consider a Grossman-Stiglitz economy: - Risky asset with true value \(v \sim N(\mu_0, \sigma_v^2 = 4)\) - \(n\) agents, fraction \(\phi\) of whom acquire a signal \(s_i = v + \varepsilon_i\), \(\varepsilon_i \sim N(0, \sigma_\varepsilon^2 = 1)\), at cost \(c = 0.5\) - Fraction \((1-\phi)\) are uninformed - Noise traders supply \(u \sim N(0, \sigma_u^2 = 1)\) units - All agents have CARA utility with \(r = 1\)

(a) In the noisy REE, the price \(p\) is a linear function of ${s}{} = {s}{} + $ noise. Write the price as \(p = A + B(v + \eta)\) where \(\eta\) is a composite noise term. Identify \(\eta\) and its variance as a function of \(\phi\), \(\sigma_\varepsilon^2\), and \(\sigma_u^2\).

(b) Compute the conditional variance of \(v\) given the equilibrium price \(p\): \(\text{Var}(v|p) = \text{Var}(v|\phi \bar{s} + \text{noise})\) as a function of \(\phi\). Show that this is decreasing in \(\phi\) (more informed traders \(\to\) more informative price).

(c) Compute the expected utility of an informed agent \(V^I(\phi)\) and of an uninformed agent \(V^U(\phi)\). Show that \(V^I\) is decreasing in \(\phi\) and \(V^U\) is increasing in \(\phi\) (use the CARA-normal expected utility formula).

(d) Find the equilibrium fraction of informed traders \(\phi^*\) by setting \(V^I(\phi^*) - c = V^U(\phi^*)\). Compute \(\phi^*\) for the given parameters.

(e) At \(\phi = \phi^*\), compute the equilibrium price informativeness: the \(R^2\) from a regression of \(v\) on \(p\). How does informativeness respond to changes in information cost \(c\)? Derive \(\frac{d\phi^*}{dc}\) and interpret.

(f) Policy exercise: Suppose a regulator mandates disclosure of all private signals (i.e., forces full revelation). Show that this cannot be an equilibrium if \(c > 0\). What happens to price informativeness and social welfare compared to the Grossman-Stiglitz equilibrium?
