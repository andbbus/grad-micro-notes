10 2.2 — Utility Representation

MWG Reference: Ch. 3, pp. 46–59

10.1 Context

Given a rational and continuous preference relation, we wish to represent it numerically — to find a real-valued function that preserves the preference ordering. The existence of such a representation is not trivial: the lexicographic example in 2.1 Preference Relations showed that rationality alone is insufficient. Debreu’s 1954 theorem establishes that rationality plus continuity is sufficient. Once the representation exists, we must grapple with its non-uniqueness: utility is ordinal, unique only up to monotone transformations, and therefore carries no cardinal or interpersonal meaning. Beyond the general existence question, consumer theory makes use of specific functional forms — quasi-linear, homothetic, CES — each of which imposes restrictions on income effects and expenditure shares that are central to structural demand estimation.

10.2 Definitions

NoteDefinition 2.2.1 — Utility Function

A function \(u: X \to \mathbb{R}\) represents the preference relation \(\succsim\) on \(X\) if for all \(x, y \in X\): \[x \succsim y \iff u(x) \geq u(y)\] Such a function is called a utility function for \(\succsim\).

CautionRemark 2.2.1 — Ordinal Utility

Utility is ordinal: if \(u\) represents \(\succsim\) and \(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) is strictly increasing, then \(v = f \circ u\) also represents \(\succsim\). Proof: \(x \succsim y \iff u(x) \geq u(y) \iff f(u(x)) \geq f(u(y)) \iff v(x) \geq v(y)\). Hence utility is unique only up to a strictly increasing transformation. This means cardinal statements (“good \(x\) gives twice the utility of \(y\)”) are meaningless; only ordinal comparisons survive. This severely limits welfare analysis: interpersonal utility comparisons and aggregate welfare measures require additional structure beyond ordinal preferences.

NoteDefinition 2.2.2 — Quasi-Linear Utility

Preferences on \(\mathbb{R}^L_+\) are quasi-linear with respect to good 1 if they can be represented by: \[u(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_L) = x_1 + v(x_2, \ldots, x_L)\] where \(v: \mathbb{R}^{L-1}_+ \to \mathbb{R}\) is a function of goods \(2\) through \(L\) only.

NoteDefinition 2.2.3 — Homothetic Preferences

Preferences \(\succsim\) on \(\mathbb{R}^L_+\) are homothetic if \(x \sim y\) implies \(\lambda x \sim \lambda y\) for all \(\lambda > 0\). Equivalently, \(\succsim\) is homothetic if it can be represented by a utility function that is homogeneous of degree one: \(u(\lambda x) = \lambda u(x)\) for all \(\lambda > 0, x \in \mathbb{R}^L_+\).

NoteDefinition 2.2.4 — CES Utility

The Constant Elasticity of Substitution (CES) utility function is: \[u(x) = \left(\sum_{l=1}^L \alpha_l x_l^\rho\right)^{1/\rho}, \quad \alpha_l > 0, \; \rho \in (-\infty, 1) \setminus \{0\}\] The elasticity of substitution between any two goods \(l\) and \(k\) is: \[\sigma = \frac{1}{1-\rho}\] Special cases: \(\rho \to 1\) gives linear (perfect substitutes, \(\sigma \to \infty\)); \(\rho \to 0\) gives Cobb-Douglas (\(\sigma = 1\)); \(\rho \to -\infty\) gives Leontief/perfect complements (\(\sigma \to 0\)).

10.3 Main Results

ImportantTheorem 2.2.1 — Debreu Representation Theorem

Let \(\succsim\) be a rational (complete and transitive) and continuous preference relation on \(\mathbb{R}^L_+\). Then there exists a continuous utility function \(u: \mathbb{R}^L_+ \to \mathbb{R}\) that represents \(\succsim\).

TipProof

We sketch the Debreu (1954) construction, which builds on the idea of using a countable dense subset of \(\mathbb{R}^L_+\) to construct utility values.

Step 1: Strong Monotonicity Simplification. For clarity, we first prove the result under strong monotonicity; the general case requires more care but uses the same architecture.

Under strong monotonicity, for any \(x \in \mathbb{R}^L_+\), consider the “diagonal ray” \(\{(t,t,\ldots,t) : t \geq 0\} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^L_+\). Since \(\succsim\) is strongly monotone, the map \(t \mapsto (t, \ldots, t)\) is strictly increasing in preference. By continuity of \(\succsim\) and the intermediate value theorem (applied to preference):

Claim: For every \(x \in \mathbb{R}^L_+\), there exists a unique \(t(x) \geq 0\) such that \(x \sim (t(x), \ldots, t(x))\).

Proof of Claim: Consider $f(t) = $ “preference comparison of \(x\) vs. \((t,\ldots,t)\).” When \(t\) is large enough (say \(t > \max_l x_l\)), strong monotonicity gives \((t,\ldots,t) \succ x\). When \(t = 0\), we have \(x \succsim (0,\ldots,0)\) by weak monotonicity. By continuity of \(\succsim\), the function \(t \mapsto \mathbf{1}[x \succsim (t,\ldots,t)]\) is upper semicontinuous and \(t \mapsto \mathbf{1}[(t,\ldots,t) \succsim x]\) is also upper semicontinuous. By a connectedness argument, there exists \(t^*\) with \(x \sim (t^*,\ldots,t^*)\). Uniqueness follows from strict monotonicity: if \(t' > t^*\) then \((t',\ldots,t') \succ (t^*,\ldots,t^*) \sim x\), so \((t',\ldots,t') \not\sim x\).

Step 2: Define \(u(x) = t(x)\). Set \(u(x) := t(x)\), the unique \(t \geq 0\) such that \(x \sim (t,\ldots,t)\).

\(u\) represents \(\succsim\): \(x \succsim y \iff (t(x),\ldots) \sim x \succsim y \sim (t(y),\ldots) \iff (t(x),\ldots) \succsim (t(y),\ldots) \iff t(x) \geq t(y)\) (by monotonicity of the diagonal).

Step 3: Continuity of \(u\). Let \(x^n \to x\). We need \(u(x^n) = t(x^n) \to t(x) = u(x)\). Let \(t_n = t(x^n)\), so \(x^n \sim (t_n,\ldots,t_n)\). Suppose \(t_n \not\to t(x)\); then there exists a subsequence \(t_{n_k} \to t' \neq t(x)\) (or diverging). By continuity of \(\succsim\) applied to \(x^{n_k} \sim (t_{n_k},\ldots,t_{n_k})\) and taking limits: \(x \sim (t', \ldots, t')\). By uniqueness, \(t' = t(x)\), contradiction. Hence \(t_n \to t(x)\), so \(u\) is continuous.

Step 4: General Case (no monotonicity). Debreu’s original proof uses a different architecture. The key insight is:

  1. \(\mathbb{R}^L_+\) is a second-countable topological space (it has a countable base). Every preference relation that is representable must have a countable “dense” subset in the preference order. More precisely, \(\succsim\) is continuous iff the topology it induces on \(X\) (generated by upper and lower contour sets) is second-countable, which holds for \(X = \mathbb{R}^L_+\).

  2. Let \(D = \mathbb{Q}^L_+ \cap X\) be the countable dense subset of \(X\). For each \(x\), the set \(D_x^- = \{d \in D : x \succ d\}\) is non-empty for most \(x\) (by density and LNS). Define \(u(x)\) via the measure of “how much of \(D\) lies below \(x\)” — formally, by assigning rational numbers to elements of \(D\) consistently with \(\succsim\) on \(D\) and then extending to all of \(X\) by: \(u(x) = \sup\{r \in \mathbb{Q} : \exists d \in D, r = u(d), d \prec x\}\).

The full Debreu proof establishes that continuity of \(\succsim\) ensures this construction yields a well-defined, continuous utility function on all of \(X\). The key step is showing continuity of \(u\): upper semicontinuity follows from the fact that \(\{x : u(x) \geq c\} = UC(d)\) for appropriately chosen \(d \in D\), and these are closed. \(\square\)

CautionRemark 2.2.2 — Necessity of Continuity

The lexicographic preference (2.1 Preference Relations, Example 2.1.1) is rational (complete and transitive) but not continuous, and has no utility representation. This shows continuity cannot be dropped from Theorem 2.2.1. In infinite-dimensional spaces, even stronger conditions are needed.

ImportantProposition 2.2.1 — Quasi-Concavity and Convex Preferences

Let \(u: \mathbb{R}^L_+ \to \mathbb{R}\) be a continuous utility function representing \(\succsim\). Then \(u\) is quasi-concave (i.e., \(\{x : u(x) \geq c\}\) is convex for all \(c \in \mathbb{R}\)) if and only if \(\succsim\) is weakly convex (Definition 2.1.9(a)).

TipProof

(\(\Rightarrow\)) Suppose \(u\) is quasi-concave. Let \(x \succsim y\) and \(z \succsim y\); we must show \(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z \succsim y\) for all \(\alpha \in [0,1]\).

Since \(x \succsim y\), we have \(u(x) \geq u(y)\). Since \(z \succsim y\), we have \(u(z) \geq u(y)\). Let \(c = u(y)\). Then \(x, z \in \{x' : u(x') \geq c\}\). By quasi-concavity, this set is convex, so \(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z \in \{x' : u(x') \geq c\}\), i.e., \(u(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z) \geq c = u(y)\). Hence \(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z \succsim y\).

(\(\Leftarrow\)) Suppose \(\succsim\) is weakly convex. Let \(c \in \mathbb{R}\) and let \(U_c = \{x : u(x) \geq c\}\) be the upper level set. We show \(U_c\) is convex. Take \(x, z \in U_c\), so \(u(x) \geq c\) and \(u(z) \geq c\).

Let \(y\) be any element with \(u(y) = c\) (if no such \(y\) exists because \(c\) is below the infimum of \(u\), then \(U_c = X\) which is convex; if \(c\) exceeds the supremum, \(U_c = \emptyset\), also convex). Actually, we work directly: \(u(x) \geq c\) means \(x \succsim y\) for any \(y\) with \(u(y) = c\). Similarly \(z \succsim y\). By weak convexity, \(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z \succsim y\), so \(u(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z) \geq u(y) = c\).

More carefully, without assuming a \(y\) exists with \(u(y) = c\): if \(u(x) \geq c\) and \(u(z) \geq c\), let \(m = \min(u(x), u(z)) \geq c\). Consider any \(y\) with \(u(y) = m\) (which is achievable by continuity of \(u\) on the path from \(x\) to \(z\) if \(u(x) \neq u(z)\)). This sub-case analysis can be avoided by noting: quasi-concavity means \(u(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z) \geq \min(u(x), u(z))\) for all \(\alpha\). We prove this directly.

Define \(v := \alpha x + (1-\alpha)z\) for \(\alpha \in (0,1)\). WLOG \(u(x) \leq u(z)\), so \(x \succsim\) (?) — actually \(u(x) \leq u(z)\) means \(z \succsim x\). Let \(c' = u(x) = \min(u(x), u(z))\). We need \(u(v) \geq c'\), i.e., \(v \succsim\) any bundle with utility \(c'\). Since \(u(x) = c'\), we need \(v \succsim x\). We have \(x \succsim x\) (trivially) and \(z \succsim x\) (since \(u(z) \geq u(x)\)). By weak convexity (taking \(y = x\)): \(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z \succsim x\), so \(u(v) \geq u(x) = c'\). Hence \(u(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z) \geq \min(u(x), u(z))\), which is exactly quasi-concavity. \(\square\)

CautionRemark 2.2.3 — Strict Quasi-Concavity

\(u\) is strictly quasi-concave if \(u(x) \geq u(y)\), \(u(z) \geq u(y)\), \(x \neq z\) implies \(u(\alpha x + (1-\alpha)z) > u(y)\) for \(\alpha \in (0,1)\). This corresponds exactly to strict convexity of preferences and ensures uniqueness of the utility-maximizing bundle given a convex budget set.

10.4 Special Functional Forms

10.4.1 Quasi-Linear Utility

\(u(x_1, \ldots, x_L) = x_1 + v(x_2, \ldots, x_L)\)

Key property: The marginal utility of wealth is constant (equal to 1), so there is no income effect on goods \(2, \ldots, L\). Specifically, if \(w\) increases by \(\Delta w\), all additional income is spent on good 1; demand for goods \(2, \ldots, L\) is unaffected. This makes quasi-linear utility ideal for settings where good 1 is a “numeraire” (e.g., money) and one studies demand for a single market good. Engel curves for goods \(2, \ldots, L\) are vertical; consumer surplus is an exact welfare measure.

10.4.2 Homothetic Preferences

Represented by a utility function homogeneous of degree one: \(u(\lambda x) = \lambda u(x)\).

Key property: Engel curves are rays through the origin — budget shares \(p_l x_l / w\) are constant in \(w\) for all \(l\). This is verified: the UMP gives \(x(p, w) = w \cdot x(p, 1)\) (by homogeneity of degree one in \(w\) from homogeneity of \(u\)). Thus demand scales proportionally with income, and expenditure shares are independent of \(w\). CES and Cobb-Douglas are homothetic; quasi-linear is not (unless \(v\) is also homogeneous).

10.4.3 CES Utility

\[u(x) = \left(\sum_{l=1}^L \alpha_l x_l^\rho\right)^{1/\rho}, \quad \rho \neq 0, \rho < 1\]

The elasticity of substitution between goods \(l\) and \(k\) is: \[\sigma_{lk} = -\frac{d \ln(x_l/x_k)}{d \ln(p_l/p_k)}\bigg|_{u = \bar{u}} = \frac{1}{1-\rho}\]

This is the same for all pairs, hence “constant” elasticity of substitution. The CES family nests: - \(\rho \to 1\): \(u(x) = \sum_l \alpha_l x_l\) (linear/perfect substitutes, \(\sigma \to \infty\)) - \(\rho \to 0\): \(u(x) = \prod_l x_l^{\alpha_l}\) (Cobb-Douglas, \(\sigma = 1\)) — derivation via L’Hopital - \(\rho \to -\infty\): \(u(x) = \min_l \{x_l/\alpha_l\}\) (Leontief/perfect complements, \(\sigma = 0\))

Cobb-Douglas derivation from CES (\(\rho \to 0\)): Take \(\ln u = \frac{1}{\rho} \ln\left(\sum_l \alpha_l x_l^\rho\right)\). As \(\rho \to 0\), applying L’Hopital: \[\lim_{\rho \to 0} \frac{\ln(\sum_l \alpha_l x_l^\rho)}{\rho} = \frac{\sum_l \alpha_l x_l^\rho \ln x_l}{\sum_l \alpha_l x_l^\rho}\bigg|_{\rho=0} = \frac{\sum_l \alpha_l \ln x_l}{\sum_l \alpha_l} = \sum_l \bar{\alpha}_l \ln x_l\] where \(\bar{\alpha}_l = \alpha_l / \sum_k \alpha_k\). So \(u \to \exp\left(\sum_l \bar{\alpha}_l \ln x_l\right) = \prod_l x_l^{\bar{\alpha}_l}\), the Cobb-Douglas form.

10.5 Economic Intuition

The Debreu theorem tells us that the preference-based and utility-function-based approaches to consumer theory are equivalent under the maintained assumptions of rationality and continuity. This equivalence justifies the standard practice of positing a utility function directly, knowing it encodes a well-defined underlying preference ordering.

The ordinal nature of utility means that two economists who use different utility representations of the same preferences (one using \(u\), another using \(\ln u\)) will derive identical demand functions, identical indifference curves, and identical behavioral predictions. The only things that can legitimately be said about utility are ordinal: “this bundle gives higher utility than that one.” Cardinal statements (“bundle \(x\) gives twice the welfare of bundle \(y\)”) are conventions imposed by the specific utility representation chosen, not features of the underlying preferences.

Special functional forms play a crucial role in applied work because they impose tractable restrictions on the shape of demand, allowing closed-form solutions and structural estimation.
