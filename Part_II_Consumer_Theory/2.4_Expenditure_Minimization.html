11  2.4 — The Expenditure Minimization Problem (EMP)

12 2.4 — The Expenditure Minimization Problem (EMP)

MWG Reference: Ch. 3, pp. 58–79

12.1 Context

The Expenditure Minimization Problem (EMP) is the dual to the UMP. While the UMP asks “what is the best bundle I can afford?”, the EMP asks “what is the cheapest way to achieve a given utility level?” The solution is the Hicksian (compensated) demand correspondence, and the value function is the expenditure function. The Hicksian demand isolates the pure substitution effect of a price change by holding utility fixed; it is the welfare-theoretically natural object for computing the compensating and equivalent variation. The expenditure function is the key tool for welfare measurement: a price change is valued by asking how much wealth must change to keep the consumer at their original utility level.

12.2 Setup and Definitions

NoteDefinition 2.4.1 — Expenditure Minimization Problem (EMP)

Given prices \(p \gg 0\) and a utility target \(\bar{u} > u(0)\) (infimum of \(u\) on \(\mathbb{R}^L_+\)), the EMP is: \[\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^L_+} \; p \cdot x \quad \text{subject to} \quad u(x) \geq \bar{u}\]

NoteDefinition 2.4.2 — Hicksian Demand Correspondence

The Hicksian (compensated) demand correspondence is: \[h(p, \bar{u}) = \arg\min_{x \geq 0, \; u(x) \geq \bar{u}} p \cdot x\] Elements of \(h(p, \bar{u})\) are the utility-compensated (Hicksian) demands.

NoteDefinition 2.4.3 — Expenditure Function

The expenditure function is the value function of the EMP: \[e(p, \bar{u}) = \min_{x \geq 0, \; u(x) \geq \bar{u}} p \cdot x = p \cdot h(p, \bar{u})\]

12.3 Existence

ImportantProposition 2.4.1 — Existence of Expenditure Minimum

If \(u\) is continuous and locally nonsatiated, and \(p \gg 0\), \(\bar{u} > u(0)\), then the EMP has a solution.

TipProof

The constraint set \(A = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^L_+ : u(x) \geq \bar{u}\}\) is closed (by continuity of \(u\)) and nonempty (by assumption, \(\bar{u}\) is attainable). The objective \(p \cdot x\) is continuous and, since \(p \gg 0\), goes to infinity as \(\|x\| \to \infty\). Hence any sequence in \(A\) along which \(p \cdot x\) approaches its infimum must be bounded; the infimum is achieved in the closure of \(A = A\). More formally, fix any \(\hat{x} \in A\) (which exists by assumption). Restrict to the compact set \(A \cap \{x : p \cdot x \leq p \cdot \hat{x}\}\), which is closed (intersection of two closed sets) and bounded (since \(p \gg 0\) bounds all components from above). The continuous function \(p \cdot x\) attains its minimum on this compact set, which equals its infimum over all of \(A\). \(\square\)

12.4 Properties of the Expenditure Function

ImportantTheorem 2.4.1 — Properties of \(e(p, \bar{u})\)

Suppose \(u\) is continuous and locally nonsatiated. The expenditure function \(e(p, \bar{u})\) satisfies: 1. Homogeneous of degree one in \(p\): \(e(\alpha p, \bar{u}) = \alpha e(p, \bar{u})\) for all \(\alpha > 0\) 2. Strictly increasing in \(\bar{u}\) 3. Nondecreasing in \(p_l\) for each \(l\) 4. Concave in \(p\) 5. Continuous in \((p, \bar{u})\)

TipProof

(1) Homogeneity of degree one: \(e(\alpha p, \bar{u}) = \min_{u(x) \geq \bar{u}} (\alpha p) \cdot x = \alpha \min_{u(x) \geq \bar{u}} p \cdot x = \alpha \, e(p, \bar{u})\). The second equality holds because \(\alpha > 0\) does not affect the argmin, only scales the objective.

(2) Strictly increasing in \(\bar{u}\): Let \(\bar{u}' > \bar{u}\). Then the constraint set \(\{x : u(x) \geq \bar{u}'\} \subsetneq \{x : u(x) \geq \bar{u}\}\) (strictly, since we can find an \(x\) with \(u(x) \in [\bar{u}, \bar{u}')\) by continuity and the assumption that \(\bar{u}'\) is achievable). Hence \(e(p, \bar{u}') \geq e(p, \bar{u})\). For strict inequality: let \(x^* \in h(p, \bar{u})\), so \(u(x^*) = \bar{u}\) (by LNS; if \(u(x^*) > \bar{u}\), we could reduce expenditure slightly while maintaining \(u(x) \geq \bar{u}\), contradicting minimality). So \(x^*\) is not in \(\{x: u(x) \geq \bar{u}'\}\), meaning the minimizer for \(\bar{u}'\) must lie in a strictly smaller set, which by LNS forces strictly higher expenditure.

(3) Nondecreasing in \(p_l\): If \(p_l\) increases to \(p_l'\), then the cost of any bundle \(x\) weakly increases (since \(x_l \geq 0\)). Hence \(e(p', \bar{u}) = \min_{u(x) \geq \bar{u}} p' \cdot x \geq \min_{u(x) \geq \bar{u}} p \cdot x = e(p, \bar{u})\).

(4) Concavity in \(p\): Let \(p, p'\) be two price vectors and \(\alpha \in [0,1]\). Let \(\tilde{p} = \alpha p + (1-\alpha)p'\). Let \(x^*\) be a minimizer for the EMP at prices \(\tilde{p}\), so \(e(\tilde{p}, \bar{u}) = \tilde{p} \cdot x^* = \alpha p \cdot x^* + (1-\alpha) p' \cdot x^*\).

Since \(u(x^*) \geq \bar{u}\), \(x^*\) is feasible for the EMP at prices \(p\) and prices \(p'\). Hence: \[p \cdot x^* \geq e(p, \bar{u}) \quad \text{and} \quad p' \cdot x^* \geq e(p', \bar{u})\] Therefore: \[e(\tilde{p}, \bar{u}) = \alpha p \cdot x^* + (1-\alpha) p' \cdot x^* \geq \alpha \, e(p, \bar{u}) + (1-\alpha) \, e(p', \bar{u})\] This is exactly concavity: \(e(\alpha p + (1-\alpha)p', \bar{u}) \geq \alpha \, e(p, \bar{u}) + (1-\alpha) \, e(p', \bar{u})\). \(\square\)

(5) Continuity: By Berge’s Maximum Theorem applied to the EMP, since the constraint correspondence \(p \mapsto \{x \geq 0 : u(x) \geq \bar{u}\}\) is constant (and thus continuous in \(p\)), and the objective \(p \cdot x\) is continuous in \((p,x)\), the value function \(e(p, \bar{u})\) is continuous in \(p\). Continuity in \(\bar{u}\) follows similarly. \(\square\)

12.5 Shephard’s Lemma

ImportantTheorem 2.4.2 — Shephard’s Lemma

If \(e(p, \bar{u})\) is differentiable in \(p\) at \((p^0, \bar{u})\), then: \[h_l(p^0, \bar{u}) = \frac{\partial e(p^0, \bar{u})}{\partial p_l} \quad \text{for all } l = 1, \ldots, L\]

TipProof

We apply the Envelope Theorem to the EMP. Write the EMP as: \[e(p, \bar{u}) = \min_x \; p \cdot x \quad \text{s.t.} \quad u(x) \geq \bar{u}\] The Lagrangian is \(\mathcal{L}(x, \mu) = p \cdot x - \mu(u(x) - \bar{u})\) where \(\mu \geq 0\).

By the envelope theorem, the derivative of the optimal value function with respect to a parameter equals the partial derivative of the Lagrangian with respect to that parameter, evaluated at the optimum: \[\frac{\partial e(p, \bar{u})}{\partial p_l} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial p_l}\bigg|_{x = h(p,\bar{u})} = x_l^* = h_l(p, \bar{u})\] since \(\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial p_l = x_l\). \(\square\)

CautionRemark 2.4.1 — Shephard’s Lemma and the Substitution Matrix

Shephard’s Lemma immediately implies: the substitution matrix (Hessian of \(e\) with respect to \(p\)): \[\frac{\partial h_l(p, \bar{u})}{\partial p_k} = \frac{\partial^2 e(p, \bar{u})}{\partial p_l \partial p_k}\] Since \(e\) is concave in \(p\), its Hessian is negative semidefinite. Since the Hessian of \(e\) is symmetric (Young’s theorem), the substitution matrix is symmetric: \(\partial h_l/\partial p_k = \partial h_k/\partial p_l\). These properties — NSD and symmetry — are the content of the Slutsky restrictions, derived more fully in 2.5 Duality and Slutsky.

12.6 Properties of Hicksian Demand

ImportantProposition 2.4.2 — Properties of Hicksian Demand

Under the maintained assumptions, Hicksian demand \(h(p, \bar{u})\) satisfies: 1. Homogeneous of degree zero in \(p\): \(h(\alpha p, \bar{u}) = h(p, \bar{u})\) for all \(\alpha > 0\) 2. The substitution matrix \(\partial h/\partial p\) is symmetric: \(\frac{\partial h_l}{\partial p_k} = \frac{\partial h_k}{\partial p_l}\) 3. The substitution matrix \(\partial h/\partial p\) is negative semidefinite: \(z^\top \frac{\partial h(p,\bar{u})}{\partial p} z \leq 0\) for all \(z \in \mathbb{R}^L\) 4. \(\frac{\partial h}{\partial p} \cdot p = 0\) (from homogeneity)

TipProof

(1) Homogeneity of degree zero: \(h(\alpha p, \bar{u}) = \arg\min_{u(x) \geq \bar{u}} (\alpha p) \cdot x = \arg\min_{u(x) \geq \bar{u}} p \cdot x = h(p, \bar{u})\), since scaling the objective by \(\alpha > 0\) does not change the argmin.

(2) Symmetry: By Shephard’s Lemma, \(h_l = \partial e / \partial p_l\) and \(h_k = \partial e/\partial p_k\). Therefore \(\partial h_l/\partial p_k = \partial^2 e / \partial p_k \partial p_l = \partial^2 e/\partial p_l \partial p_k = \partial h_k/\partial p_l\) by Young’s theorem (equality of mixed partials), valid when \(e\) is twice continuously differentiable.

(3) Negative semidefiniteness: Since \(e(p, \bar{u})\) is concave in \(p\) (Theorem 2.4.1(4)), its Hessian \(D^2_p e = \partial h/\partial p\) is negative semidefinite. More explicitly: by concavity, for any vector \(z\): \[e(p + tz, \bar{u}) \leq e(p, \bar{u}) + t \nabla_p e \cdot z + \frac{t^2}{2} z^\top D^2_p e \cdot z\] but also from concavity: \(e(p + tz, \bar{u}) \leq e(p, \bar{u}) + t z^\top \nabla_p e(p, \bar{u})\) is not directly useful. The correct statement: \(e\) is concave iff its Hessian is NSD wherever it exists. Hence \(D^2_p e \preceq 0\), i.e., \(z^\top D^2_p e \, z \leq 0\) for all \(z\).

(4) \((\partial h/\partial p) p = 0\): Differentiating the homogeneity condition \(h(\alpha p, \bar{u}) = h(p, \bar{u})\) with respect to \(\alpha\) at \(\alpha = 1\) yields \(\frac{\partial h(p, \bar{u})}{\partial p} p = 0\) (by Euler’s theorem for homogeneous functions of degree zero). \(\square\)

CautionRemark 2.4.2 — No Income Effect

Hicksian demand holds utility constant by construction. Therefore, it has no income effect: changes in \(p\) affect \(h\) only through the substitution channel. The own-price effect satisfies \(\partial h_l/\partial p_l \leq 0\) (from NSD of the substitution matrix — set \(z = e_l\)), meaning Hicksian demand is always downward sloping. This contrasts with Marshallian demand, which can slope upward for Giffen goods (where a strong income effect reverses the substitution effect).

12.7 Economic Intuition

The EMP is the “cost side” of consumer theory. It answers a dual question to the UMP: instead of maximizing utility within a budget, it minimizes cost while meeting a utility standard. The Hicksian demand isolates the pure substitution response to a price change, holding the consumer’s welfare fixed. This makes it the correct object for welfare measurement: if good \(l\)’s price rises, the Hicksian demand shows how the consumer would substitute away from \(l\) if they were compensated enough to maintain their original utility level.

The concavity of \(e\) in \(p\) has a transparent revealed-preference interpretation: if a firm (or consumer) can substitute away from goods whose prices rise, total costs are “convex” — but if the cost function is evaluated at a fixed bundle, costs are linear in prices. The minimum cost is therefore always below or equal to the cost of the fixed bundle, making the minimum-cost function concave.

Shephard’s Lemma is the dual analog of Roy’s Identity: just as the Walrasian demand can be recovered from the indirect utility function by differentiation, the Hicksian demand can be recovered from the expenditure function by differentiation. This provides a remarkably clean connection between welfare (expenditure) and observable demand.
