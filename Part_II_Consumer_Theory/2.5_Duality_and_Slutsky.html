12  2.5 — Duality and the Slutsky Equation

13 2.5 — Duality and the Slutsky Equation

MWG Reference: Ch. 3, pp. 67–88

13.1 Context

The duality between the UMP and EMP is the central organizing principle of consumer theory. The two problems — maximizing utility subject to a budget constraint, and minimizing expenditure to achieve a utility target — are mathematically dual, and their value functions and solution correspondences are tightly linked. The Slutsky equation is the crown jewel of this duality: it decomposes the total price effect on Walrasian demand into a substitution effect (measured by Hicksian demand) and an income effect. This decomposition is indispensable for welfare analysis and demand estimation, since it separates the welfare-neutral reallocation effect (substitution) from the wealth effect (income). The Slutsky matrix must be symmetric and negative semidefinite — properties that serve as the key testable restrictions on any demand system.

13.2 Duality Relations

ImportantTheorem 2.5.1 — The Four Duality Identities

Let \(u\) be continuous and locally nonsatiated, \(p \gg 0\), \(w > 0\), \(\bar{u} \in \text{range}(v(p, \cdot))\). Then: 1. \(e(p, v(p,w)) = w\) 2. \(v(p, e(p,\bar{u})) = \bar{u}\) 3. \(x(p,w) = h(p, v(p,w))\) 4. \(h(p,\bar{u}) = x(p, e(p,\bar{u}))\)

TipProof

(1) \(e(p, v(p,w)) = w\): Let \(x^* = x(p,w)\), so \(v(p,w) = u(x^*)\) and \(p \cdot x^* = w\) (by Walras’ Law). We claim \(x^*\) also solves the EMP at \(\bar{u} = v(p,w)\). Feasibility: \(u(x^*) = v(p,w) = \bar{u}\). Optimality: suppose some \(y\) with \(u(y) \geq \bar{u} = u(x^*)\) had \(p \cdot y < p \cdot x^* = w\). Then \(y \in B(p,w)\) and \(u(y) \geq u(x^*)\). By local nonsatiation, we can find \(y'\) near \(y\) with \(u(y') > u(x^*)\) and \(y' \in B(p,w)\), contradicting optimality of \(x^*\) in the UMP. Hence \(x^*\) is a solution to the EMP at \(\bar{u} = v(p,w)\), and \(e(p, v(p,w)) = p \cdot x^* = w\).

(2) \(v(p, e(p,\bar{u})) = \bar{u}\): Let \(h^* = h(p, \bar{u})\), so \(e(p, \bar{u}) = p \cdot h^*\) and \(u(h^*) \geq \bar{u}\). We show \(h^*\) also solves the UMP at wealth \(w = e(p, \bar{u})\). Feasibility: \(p \cdot h^* = e(p, \bar{u}) = w\). Optimality: suppose some \(y \in B(p, w)\) had \(u(y) > u(h^*)\). Then \(u(y) > \bar{u}\) (since \(u(h^*) \geq \bar{u}\), so \(u(y) > u(h^*) \geq \bar{u}\)), making \(y\) feasible for the EMP at \(\bar{u}\). But \(p \cdot y \leq w = p \cdot h^* = e(p, \bar{u})\), contradicting the minimality of \(h^*\) in the EMP (by LNS, equality holds, so \(p \cdot y = e(p,\bar{u})\) and we can find a cheaper nearby bundle with \(u \geq \bar{u}\), contradiction). Hence \(h^*\) solves the UMP at \(e(p, \bar{u})\). Therefore \(v(p, e(p, \bar{u})) = u(h^*) = \bar{u}\) (the last equality: \(u(h^*) = \bar{u}\) by LNS — if \(u(h^*) > \bar{u}\), we could find a cheaper feasible bundle, contradicting optimality of \(h^*\)).

(3) \(x(p,w) = h(p, v(p,w))\): Let \(\bar{u} = v(p,w)\). From (1), \(e(p, \bar{u}) = w\). From (4) (proven below independently), \(h(p, \bar{u}) = x(p, e(p, \bar{u})) = x(p, w)\).

Direct proof: Let \(x^* = x(p,w)\). We showed in (1) that \(x^*\) solves the EMP at \(\bar{u} = v(p,w)\). Hence \(x^* \in h(p, v(p,w))\), i.e., \(x(p,w) = h(p, v(p,w))\).

(4) \(h(p,\bar{u}) = x(p, e(p,\bar{u}))\): Let \(h^* = h(p, \bar{u})\) and \(w = e(p, \bar{u}) = p \cdot h^*\). We showed in (2) that \(h^*\) solves the UMP at wealth \(w\). Hence \(h^* \in x(p, e(p, \bar{u}))\), i.e., \(h(p, \bar{u}) = x(p, e(p, \bar{u}))\). \(\square\)

13.3 The Slutsky Equation

ImportantTheorem 2.5.2 — The Slutsky Equation

Suppose \(x(p,w)\) and \(h(p, \bar{u})\) are differentiable. For any \((p, w)\) with \(v(p,w) = \bar{u}\): \[\frac{\partial x_l(p,w)}{\partial p_k} = \frac{\partial h_l(p,\bar{u})}{\partial p_k} - x_k(p,w) \frac{\partial x_l(p,w)}{\partial w}\] In matrix form: \(Dx_p(p,w) = S(p,w) - x(p,w) \cdot Dx_w(p,w)^\top\), where \(S_{lk} = \partial h_l/\partial p_k\) is the Slutsky matrix.

TipProof

We differentiate the duality identity (4): \(h_l(p, \bar{u}) = x_l(p, e(p, \bar{u}))\) with respect to \(p_k\).

The right-hand side is a composite function of \(p_k\) through two channels: directly through the first argument of \(x_l\), and through the second argument \(e(p, \bar{u})\) which depends on \(p_k\) via Shephard’s Lemma. Applying the chain rule: \[\frac{\partial h_l(p, \bar{u})}{\partial p_k} = \frac{\partial x_l(p, e(p,\bar{u}))}{\partial p_k}\bigg|_w + \frac{\partial x_l(p, e(p,\bar{u}))}{\partial w} \cdot \frac{\partial e(p, \bar{u})}{\partial p_k}\] The partial derivative on the right (first term) holds \(w = e(p, \bar{u})\) fixed, so it is \(\partial x_l(p,w)/\partial p_k\). By Shephard’s Lemma, \(\partial e/\partial p_k = h_k(p, \bar{u}) = x_k(p,w)\) (using identity (3)). Substituting: \[\frac{\partial h_l}{\partial p_k} = \frac{\partial x_l}{\partial p_k} + \frac{\partial x_l}{\partial w} \cdot x_k(p,w)\] Rearranging: \[\frac{\partial x_l(p,w)}{\partial p_k} = \frac{\partial h_l(p,\bar{u})}{\partial p_k} - x_k(p,w) \frac{\partial x_l(p,w)}{\partial w} \qquad \square\]

CautionRemark 2.5.1 — Interpretation of the Slutsky Equation

The Slutsky equation decomposes the total price effect \(\partial x_l/\partial p_k\) into: - Substitution effect \(\partial h_l/\partial p_k\): the change in demand for \(l\) when \(p_k\) changes and the consumer is compensated to remain at the same utility level. This is always non-positive for own-price changes (\(l = k\)). - Income effect \(-x_k \cdot \partial x_l/\partial w\): the change in demand for \(l\) due to the reduction in real wealth caused by the price increase. The consumer purchased \(x_k\) units of good \(k\), so a rise of \(dp_k\) reduces effective wealth by \(x_k \, dp_k\).

13.4 The Slutsky Matrix

NoteDefinition 2.5.1 — Slutsky Matrix

The Slutsky matrix is the \(L \times L\) matrix of substitution effects: \[S(p,w) = \left[\frac{\partial h_l(p,v(p,w))}{\partial p_k}\right]_{l,k} = \left[\frac{\partial x_l}{\partial p_k} + x_k \frac{\partial x_l}{\partial w}\right]_{l,k}\] The second expression shows that \(S\) can be constructed entirely from the observable Walrasian demand and its derivatives.

ImportantProposition 2.5.1 — Properties of the Slutsky Matrix

Under the maintained assumptions, \(S(p,w)\) is: 1. Symmetric: \(S_{lk} = S_{kl}\) 2. Negative semidefinite: \(z^\top S(p,w) z \leq 0\) for all \(z \in \mathbb{R}^L\) 3. \(S(p,w) p = 0\) (Slutsky matrix times \(p\) is zero)

TipProof

(1) Symmetry: \(S_{lk} = \partial h_l/\partial p_k = \partial^2 e/\partial p_l \partial p_k\) (by Shephard’s Lemma). By Young’s theorem, \(\partial^2 e/\partial p_l \partial p_k = \partial^2 e/\partial p_k \partial p_l = S_{kl}\).

(2) NSD: Since \(e(p, \bar{u})\) is concave in \(p\) (Theorem 2.4.1(4)), its Hessian \(D^2_p e\) is negative semidefinite. By Shephard’s Lemma, \(D^2_p e = \partial h/\partial p = S\). Hence \(z^\top S z \leq 0\) for all \(z\).

Alternatively, for own-price effects: \(S_{ll} = \partial h_l/\partial p_l \leq 0\) because \(h_l\) is the minimizer of a cost problem and the own-price effect of the compensated demand is non-positive (complementary slackness and the cost-minimization first-order conditions).

(3) \(Sp = 0\): Differentiate the homogeneity condition \(h(\alpha p, \bar{u}) = h(p, \bar{u})\) with respect to \(\alpha\) at \(\alpha = 1\): \[\sum_k \frac{\partial h_l}{\partial p_k} p_k = 0 \quad \text{for all } l\] This is \([S \cdot p]_l = 0\) for all \(l\), i.e., \(Sp = 0\). \(\square\)

CautionRemark 2.5.2 — NSD and Giffen Goods

\(S_{ll} \leq 0\) means the own-price Hicksian (compensated) effect is non-positive: compensated own-price demand curves slope downward. For Walrasian (Marshallian) demand, the own-price effect is \(\partial x_l/\partial p_l = S_{ll} - x_l \cdot \partial x_l/\partial w\). If good \(l\) is inferior (\(\partial x_l/\partial w < 0\)), then the income effect \(-x_l \cdot \partial x_l / \partial w > 0\) works against the negative substitution effect. If this income effect dominates, \(\partial x_l/\partial p_l > 0\): demand rises with price — a Giffen good. This requires inferiority plus a sufficiently strong income effect.

13.5 Income and Substitution Effects

NoteDefinition 2.5.2 — Normal and Inferior Goods

Good \(l\) is normal at \((p,w)\) if \(\partial x_l(p,w)/\partial w > 0\) (demand increases with wealth). It is inferior if \(\partial x_l/\partial w < 0\).

NoteDefinition 2.5.3 — Complements and Substitutes

Goods \(l\) and \(k\) are net complements if \(S_{lk} = \partial h_l/\partial p_k < 0\) (compensated demand for \(l\) falls when \(p_k\) rises). They are net substitutes if \(S_{lk} > 0\).

Note: By symmetry of \(S\), the relationship is symmetric: \(l\) is a net substitute for \(k\) iff \(k\) is a net substitute for \(l\).

13.6 Compensating and Equivalent Variation

NoteDefinition 2.5.4 — Compensating Variation

The Compensating Variation (CV) of a price change from \(p^0\) to \(p^1\) (with wealth \(w\)) is: \[CV = e(p^0, \bar{u}^0) - e(p^1, \bar{u}^0) = w - e(p^1, v(p^0, w))\] where \(\bar{u}^0 = v(p^0, w)\). CV is the amount of wealth that must be given to the consumer after the price change to restore the original utility level.

NoteDefinition 2.5.5 — Equivalent Variation

The Equivalent Variation (EV) of a price change from \(p^0\) to \(p^1\) is: \[EV = e(p^1, \bar{u}^1) - e(p^0, \bar{u}^1) = e(p^1, v(p^1, w)) - e(p^0, v(p^1, w)) = w - e(p^0, v(p^1, w))\] where \(\bar{u}^1 = v(p^1, w)\). EV is the amount of wealth that would make the consumer indifferent between the price change and receiving the wealth transfer at old prices.

CautionRemark 2.5.3 — CV, EV, and Consumer Surplus

For a single price change in good \(l\) from \(p_l^0\) to \(p_l^1\): \[CV = -\int_{p_l^0}^{p_l^1} h_l(p_l, p_{-l}, \bar{u}^0) \, dp_l, \qquad EV = -\int_{p_l^0}^{p_l^1} h_l(p_l, p_{-l}, \bar{u}^1) \, dp_l\] The area under the ordinary (Marshallian) demand curve (consumer surplus) lies between CV and EV (for normal goods). CV and EV coincide with consumer surplus only in the quasi-linear case (no income effects).

13.7 Empirical Link: Slutsky Symmetry as a Testable Restriction

The Slutsky symmetry conditions \(S_{lk} = S_{kl}\) are the key testable restrictions on demand systems. In applied demand analysis, one estimates a demand system \(x(p,w)\) and tests whether the implied Slutsky matrix is symmetric and NSD. These restrictions reduce the number of free parameters in a flexible demand system (e.g., AIDS, QUAIDS) by imposing cross-equation restrictions that can be tested with standard Wald, LR, or LM tests. If symmetry is rejected, the estimated demand system is not rationalizable by any well-behaved utility function, which has implications for the validity of welfare calculations based on CV and EV.

13.8 Economic Intuition

The Slutsky decomposition gives a complete picture of how demand responds to price changes. The substitution effect always works in the expected direction: a price increase for good \(l\) leads to substitution away from \(l\) toward other goods. The income effect depends on whether the good is normal (strengthening the demand reduction) or inferior (partially or wholly counteracting it). A Giffen good requires a very strong inferiority: the income loss from the higher price of a good that constitutes a large share of spending must swamp the substitution effect. Such goods are theoretically possible but empirically rare.

The symmetry of \(S\) reflects the underlying structure of preferences: the marginal willingness to substitute \(l\) for \(k\) (at constant utility) is the same as the marginal willingness to substitute \(k\) for \(l\). This symmetry is a restriction that preferences impose on the demand system — if it is violated in data, the data cannot be rationalized by any well-behaved utility function.
