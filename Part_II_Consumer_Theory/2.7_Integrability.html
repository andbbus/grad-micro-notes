15 2.7 — Integrability

MWG Reference: Ch. 3, pp. 88–101

15.1 Context

The integrability problem asks the inverse question to the UMP: given an observed demand system \(x(p,w)\), does there exist a utility function that rationalizes it? The utility maximization problem tells us that demand derived from a well-behaved utility function must satisfy certain properties: homogeneity of degree zero, Walras’ Law, and Slutsky symmetry plus NSD. The integrability theorem (Hurwicz-Uzawa) establishes that these conditions are not only necessary but also sufficient for local (and, under appropriate conditions, global) rationalizability. This gives a clean characterization of rational demand systems. The integrability problem is also practically important: given estimated demand parameters, one can use integrability to recover the expenditure function and perform welfare calculations (CV, EV) that are consistent with an underlying preference ordering.

15.2 The Integrability Problem

NoteDefinition 2.7.1 — Rationalizability

A demand function \(x: \mathbb{R}^L_{++} \times \mathbb{R}_{++} \to \mathbb{R}^L_+\) is rationalizable if there exists a locally nonsatiated utility function \(u: \mathbb{R}^L_+ \to \mathbb{R}\) such that \(x(p,w)\) solves the UMP \(\max_{p \cdot y \leq w} u(y)\) for all \((p,w)\).

15.3 Necessary Conditions

ImportantProposition 2.7.1 — Necessary Conditions for Rationalizability

If \(x(p,w)\) is the Walrasian demand derived from a locally nonsatiated, differentiable utility function, then \(x(p,w)\) satisfies: 1. Homogeneity of degree zero: \(x(\alpha p, \alpha w) = x(p,w)\) for all \(\alpha > 0\) 2. Walras’ Law: \(p \cdot x(p,w) = w\) for all \((p,w)\) 3. Slutsky symmetry: \(S_{lk}(p,w) = S_{kl}(p,w)\) for all \(l, k\), where \(S_{lk} = \partial x_l/\partial p_k + x_k \partial x_l/\partial w\) 4. Slutsky NSD: the Slutsky matrix \(S(p,w)\) is negative semidefinite

TipProof

  1. and (2) were proven in 2.3 Utility Maximization Problem (Propositions 2.3.3 and 2.3.4).

  2. and (4) were proven in 2.5 Duality and Slutsky (Proposition 2.5.1): \(S = \partial h/\partial p\) and the Hessian of the concave expenditure function \(e\) is symmetric (Young’s theorem) and NSD (concavity). \(\square\)

15.4 The Integrability Theorem

ImportantTheorem 2.7.1 — Hurwicz-Uzawa Integrability Theorem

Let \(x: \mathbb{R}^L_{++} \times \mathbb{R}_{++} \to \mathbb{R}^L_+\) be a \(C^1\) demand function satisfying: 1. Homogeneity of degree zero in \((p,w)\) 2. Walras’ Law: \(p \cdot x(p,w) = w\) 3. The Slutsky matrix \(S(p,w)\) is symmetric and negative semidefinite

Then \(x(p,w)\) is locally rationalizable: for every \((p^0, w^0)\), there exists a neighborhood \(U\) of \((p^0, w^0)\) and a locally nonsatiated utility function \(u\) such that \(x(p,w)\) solves the UMP on \(U\).

If additionally \(S(p,w)\) is everywhere NSD (not just locally) and the system satisfies appropriate global conditions, the rationalization is global.

TipProof

Step 1: From demand to Hicksian demand and the expenditure function. The strategy is to construct \(e(p, \bar{u})\) by integrating the ODE system implied by Shephard’s Lemma: \(\partial e/\partial p_l = h_l(p, \bar{u})\).

Fix a utility level \(\bar{u}\) and initial prices \(p^0\). Define the Hicksian demand implicitly: by duality (Theorem 2.5.1), \(h(p, \bar{u}) = x(p, e(p, \bar{u}))\). Substituting into Shephard’s Lemma, we get a system of ODEs: \[\frac{\partial e(p, \bar{u})}{\partial p_l} = x_l(p, e(p, \bar{u})), \quad l = 1, \ldots, L\] with initial condition \(e(p^0, \bar{u}) = w^0\) (where \(v(p^0, w^0) = \bar{u}\)).

Step 2: Integrability condition (Frobenius theorem). The ODE system \(\partial e/\partial p_l = x_l(p, e)\) has a (locally unique) solution if and only if the system is integrable — i.e., the cross-partial conditions are satisfied: \[\frac{\partial^2 e}{\partial p_k \partial p_l} = \frac{\partial^2 e}{\partial p_l \partial p_k}\] Substituting the ODE: \(\partial^2 e/\partial p_k \partial p_l = \partial x_l/\partial p_k + x_k \partial x_l/\partial e = \partial x_l/\partial p_k + x_k \partial x_l/\partial w\) (since \(\partial e/\partial p_k = x_k\), and \(\partial x_l/\partial e = \partial x_l/\partial w\)).

The integrability condition becomes: \[\frac{\partial x_l}{\partial p_k} + x_k \frac{\partial x_l}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial x_k}{\partial p_l} + x_l \frac{\partial x_k}{\partial w}\] i.e., \(S_{lk} = S_{kl}\): Slutsky symmetry. Thus, by the Frobenius theorem on integrability of PDE systems, the existence of a solution to the ODE system is equivalent to Slutsky symmetry.

Step 3: Constructing the utility function. Once we have the expenditure function \(e(p, \bar{u})\) (constructed by solving the ODE), we define the utility function via its upper contour sets. For each wealth level \(w\) and price vector \(p\), define: \[u(x) = \max\{\bar{u} : e(p, \bar{u}) \leq p \cdot x \text{ for some } p \text{ supporting } x\}\] Under appropriate regularity (which follows from NSD of \(S\) ensuring \(e\) is concave in \(p\) and thus well-behaved), this construction yields a valid utility function. Alternatively, one can use the expenditure function to define preferences directly via indifference curves.

Step 4: Verification. One must verify that the constructed \(u\) rationalizes the original demand \(x(p,w)\). This follows from the duality identities: if \(e(p, \bar{u})\) satisfies Shephard’s Lemma with \(h_l = x_l(p, e)\), then the demand system derived from maximizing \(u\) (defined from \(e\)) recovers \(x(p,w)\). The NSD condition ensures \(e\) is concave, which is needed for \(u\) to be quasi-concave. \(\square\)

CautionRemark 2.7.1 — The Frobenius Theorem

The Frobenius theorem states that a system of PDEs \(\partial f/\partial x_i = g_i(x, f)\) has a local solution if and only if the integrability conditions \(\partial g_i/\partial x_j + g_j \partial g_i/\partial f = \partial g_j/\partial x_i + g_i \partial g_j/\partial f\) hold (i.e., the vector field \((g_i)\) is integrable). In our application, \(f = e\), \(x = p\), \(g_l = x_l(p, e)\), and the integrability condition reduces exactly to Slutsky symmetry.

15.5 Recovering the Expenditure Function from Hicksian Demand

Given \(h(p, \bar{u})\) satisfying Shephard’s conditions (homogeneity of degree zero in \(p\), symmetry, NSD), we recover \(e(p, \bar{u})\) by solving: \[\frac{d}{dt} e(p(t), \bar{u}) = \sum_l \frac{\partial e}{\partial p_l} \dot{p}_l(t) = \sum_l h_l(p(t), \bar{u}) \dot{p}_l(t)\] along any path \(p(t)\) from \(p^0\) to \(p\). The symmetry of \(S = \partial h/\partial p\) (Slutsky symmetry) guarantees path-independence of this line integral. Hence: \[e(p, \bar{u}) = e(p^0, \bar{u}) + \int_{p^0}^{p} h(q, \bar{u}) \cdot dq\] where the integral is along any path from \(p^0\) to \(p\).

NoteExample 2.7.1 — Recovering Utility from a Two-Good Demand System

Suppose \(L = 2\) and we observe: \[x_1(p, w) = \frac{\alpha w}{p_1}, \qquad x_2(p, w) = \frac{(1-\alpha) w}{p_2}\] for some \(\alpha \in (0,1)\). (This is the Walrasian demand for Cobb-Douglas utility \(u = x_1^\alpha x_2^{1-\alpha}\).)

Step 1: Verify necessary conditions. - Homogeneity: \(x_l(\alpha p, \alpha w) = \alpha w/(\alpha p_l) \cdot [\text{appropriate factor}]\): \(x_1(\lambda p, \lambda w) = \alpha \lambda w / (\lambda p_1) = \alpha w/p_1 = x_1(p,w)\). ✓ - Walras’ Law: \(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 = \alpha w + (1-\alpha) w = w\). ✓ - Slutsky matrix: \(S_{11} = \partial x_1/\partial p_1 + x_1 \partial x_1/\partial w = -\alpha w/p_1^2 + (\alpha w/p_1) \cdot (\alpha/p_1) = -\alpha w/p_1^2 + \alpha^2 w/p_1^2 = -\alpha(1-\alpha)w/p_1^2 \leq 0\). ✓ - Cross-Slutsky: \(S_{12} = \partial x_1/\partial p_2 + x_2 \partial x_1/\partial w = 0 + \frac{(1-\alpha)w}{p_2} \cdot \frac{\alpha}{p_1} = \frac{\alpha(1-\alpha)w}{p_1 p_2}\). - \(S_{21} = \partial x_2/\partial p_1 + x_1 \partial x_2/\partial w = 0 + \frac{\alpha w}{p_1} \cdot \frac{1-\alpha}{p_2} = \frac{\alpha(1-\alpha)w}{p_1 p_2} = S_{12}\). Symmetry ✓.

Step 2: Integrate to find \(e(p, \bar{u})\). The ODE system is: \(\partial e/\partial p_l = h_l(p, \bar{u}) = x_l(p, e) = \alpha e/p_1\) (for \(l=1\)) and \((1-\alpha)e/p_2\) (for \(l=2\)).

This is a system of linear ODEs. Integrating: \(d(\ln e) = \alpha \, d(\ln p_1) + (1-\alpha) d(\ln p_2)\), which integrates to \(\ln e = \alpha \ln p_1 + (1-\alpha) \ln p_2 + C(\bar{u})\), or: \[e(p, \bar{u}) = A(\bar{u}) \cdot p_1^\alpha p_2^{1-\alpha}\] where \(A(\bar{u})\) is determined by the initial condition. Setting \(e(p, v(p,w)) = w\): \(A(v(p,w)) = w/(p_1^\alpha p_2^{1-\alpha})\). Since \(v(p,w) = u(x(p,w)) = ({\alpha w}/{p_1})^\alpha \cdot ({(1-\alpha)w}/{p_2})^{1-\alpha} = w \cdot \alpha^\alpha(1-\alpha)^{1-\alpha}/p_1^\alpha p_2^{1-\alpha}\), we get \(A(\bar{u}) = \bar{u}/(\alpha^\alpha(1-\alpha)^{1-\alpha})\).

Therefore: \[e(p, \bar{u}) = \frac{\bar{u}}{\alpha^\alpha(1-\alpha)^{1-\alpha}} \cdot p_1^\alpha p_2^{1-\alpha}\] This is the correct Cobb-Douglas expenditure function, confirming that the demand system is rationalized by \(u = x_1^\alpha x_2^{1-\alpha}\).

15.6 Practical Implications for Structural Demand Estimation

The integrability theorem has a crucial practical consequence for structural demand estimation. In applied work, one typically:

  1. Specify a demand system \(x(p,w; \theta)\) with parameters \(\theta\) — e.g., the Almost Ideal Demand System (AIDS) of Deaton-Muellbauer.
  2. Estimate \(\theta\) from price and quantity data.
  3. Impose integrability restrictions: Slutsky symmetry (\(S_{lk} = S_{kl}\)) as cross-equation restrictions; homogeneity of degree zero as within-equation restrictions; NSD as inequality restrictions.
  4. Recover the expenditure function by integrating the estimated Hicksian demand system.
  5. Compute welfare measures (CV, EV) from the recovered expenditure function.

Step 3 is possible precisely because of the Hurwicz-Uzawa theorem: if the estimated demand satisfies Slutsky symmetry, homogeneity, and NSD, then an underlying utility function exists, and welfare calculations are internally consistent (i.e., they correspond to actual preference-based comparisons). Without imposing integrability, welfare measures derived from the estimated demand system may be internally inconsistent.

CautionRemark 2.7.2 — Global vs. Local Integrability

The Hurwicz-Uzawa theorem guarantees local integrability. Global integrability — the existence of a single utility function rationalizing demand everywhere — requires additional conditions ensuring the ODE solutions are well-defined globally and that the resulting \(e(p, \bar{u})\) is globally well-behaved. In practice, parametric demand systems (AIDS, QUAIDS) are designed to be globally integrable by construction, with the integrability conditions imposed as parameter restrictions.
