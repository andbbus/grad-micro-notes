58.1 Problem 1 — Majority Rule: Arrow’s Axioms and Condorcet Cycles

Let \(X = \{a, b, c\}\) and \(I = \{1, 2, 3\}\) (three voters). Majority rule \(F^{maj}\) is defined as follows: \(x R^{maj} y\) iff \(|\{i : x R_i y\}| \geq |\{i : y R_i x\}|\) (alternative \(x\) is socially at least as good as \(y\) if it has at least as many supporters).

Consider the following Condorcet profile: - Voter 1: \(a P_1 b P_1 c\) - Voter 2: \(b P_2 c P_2 a\) - Voter 3: \(c P_3 a P_3 b\)

(a) Compute the majority ranking for each pair: \((a,b)\), \((b,c)\), and \((a,c)\). Show that the social relation is not transitive (it exhibits a Condorcet cycle). Which of Arrow’s axioms does \(F^{maj}\) violate on this profile?

(b) Verify that \(F^{maj}\) satisfies axioms P (Pareto), IIA, and ND for the Condorcet profile. Show the calculations for each axiom check.

(c) Now consider the profile: - Voter 1: \(a P_1 b P_1 c\) - Voter 2: \(a P_2 c P_2 b\) - Voter 3: \(b P_3 c P_3 a\)

Compute the majority ranking for all pairs. Is the social ordering transitive in this case? Identify the Condorcet winner (if it exists).

(d) A preference profile satisfies the single-peaked condition over a linear order \(\succ^*\) on \(X\) if for each voter \(i\), there exists a “peak” \(p(i) \in X\) such that: if \(x \succ^* p(i) \succ^* y\) or \(y \succ^* p(i) \succ^* x\), then \(x P_i y\) iff \(x\) is closer to \(p(i)\). Formally define single-peaked preferences.

Check whether the profile in (c) is single-peaked over the order \(a \succ^* b \succ^* c\). Apply Black’s Median Voter Theorem to identify the majority rule winner.

(e) Prove that for any profile of single-peaked preferences over a linear order on \(X\), majority rule is transitive. (This is the key theorem showing that restricting the domain to single-peaked preferences satisfies all four Arrow axioms.) Identify precisely which step of Arrow’s impossibility proof fails under this domain restriction.

58.2 Problem 2 — Arrow’s Theorem for \(|X| = 3\), \(|I| = 2\)

Let \(X = \{x, y, z\}\) and \(I = \{1, 2\}\). You will prove Arrow’s impossibility theorem directly for this minimal case.

(a) List all logically possible strict preference orderings over \(X\) (there are 6). List all possible profiles \((R_1, R_2) \in \mathcal{R}^2\) (there are \(6 \times 6 = 36\); you may work symbolically).

(b) Suppose \(F\) satisfies axioms U, P, and IIA. Consider the profile: \[R_1: x P_1 y P_1 z, \quad R_2: y P_2 z P_2 x.\] By Pareto, we must have \(y P^S z\). By IIA, the social ranking of \((x, z)\) depends only on how individuals rank \(x\) vs. \(z\). Since \(x P_1 z\) and \(z P_2 x\), write \(F^{xz}(1,2) \in \{xPz, zPx, xIz\}\) for the social ranking.

(c) Consider the two profiles: - Profile A: \(R_1: x P_1 y P_1 z\); \(R_2: x P_2 y P_2 z\) - Profile B: \(R_1: x P_1 y P_1 z\); \(R_2: y P_2 x P_2 z\)

In Profile A, Pareto requires \(x P^S y\) and \(x P^S z\) and \(y P^S z\). In Profile B, both agree that \(x P y\) is not unanimous. Show via IIA applied across several profiles that the social ranking of \((x,y)\) in Profile B cannot be \(y P^S x\) if \(F\) satisfies P and IIA.

(d) Systematically, using profiles that vary one individual’s preference at a time, show that either individual 1 is decisive for every pair of alternatives, or individual 2 is. (Hint: use the “pivotal voter” method: find the profile where flipping individual \(k\)’s preference changes the social ranking, and show \(k\) is decisive.)

(e) Conclude that \(F\) must be a dictatorship, contradicting ND. Write out the full logical chain of implications from steps (b)–(d) to the contradiction.

58.3 Problem 3 — Scitovsky Reversal: An Explicit Counterexample

Consider a two-agent economy with two goods \((q_1, q_2)\) and total endowment \((10, 10)\). Agents have quasi-linear utilities: \[u_1(q_{11}, q_{12}) = q_{11} + v_1(q_{12}), \quad u_2(q_{21}, q_{22}) = q_{21} + v_2(q_{22})\] where \(v_1(q) = 4\sqrt{q}\) and \(v_2(q) = q^2/8\).

State A: Agent 1 gets \((5, 8)\), Agent 2 gets \((5, 2)\).

State B: Agent 1 gets \((5, 2)\), Agent 2 gets \((5, 8)\).

(a) Compute the utilities \(u_1^A, u_2^A, u_1^B, u_2^B\). Is either state Pareto superior to the other?

(b) Compute the compensating variation (CV) for each agent from the move \(A \to B\): - \(CV_i\) is the income change that makes agent \(i\) indifferent between \(A\) (with \(CV_i\) income transfer) and \(B\). - More precisely, \(CV_i\) solves \(u_i^B = u_i^A + CV_i\) (using quasi-linearity).

(c) Check whether \(B \succ_{KH} A\) by computing \(\sum_i CV_i\) for the move \(A \to B\).

(d) Check whether \(A \succ_{KH} B\) by computing \(\sum_i CV_i\) for the move \(B \to A\) (i.e., \(-(CV_i)\) from (b)).

(e) If both \(B \succ_{KH} A\) and \(A \succ_{KH} B\), you have demonstrated a Scitovsky reversal. Explain geometrically why this occurs: draw the “community indifference curves” for states \(A\) and \(B\) and show they intersect.

(f) Discuss the policy implication: a government that uses the Kaldor-Hicks criterion to evaluate a policy change may simultaneously justify both implementing and reversing the policy. What does Samuelson’s critique add to this observation?

58.4 Problem 4 — Borda Count and Violation of IIA

Consider three voters \(\{1, 2, 3\}\) and four alternatives \(X = \{a, b, c, d\}\).

The Borda count assigns to each alternative a score equal to the number of alternatives ranked below it, summed across voters. The social ordering ranks alternatives by total Borda score.

Profile 1: - Voter 1: \(a \succ b \succ c \succ d\) (scores: \(a=3, b=2, c=1, d=0\)) - Voter 2: \(b \succ a \succ c \succ d\) (scores: \(a=2, b=3, c=1, d=0\)) - Voter 3: \(b \succ c \succ a \succ d\) (scores: \(a=1, b=3, c=2, d=0\))

(a) Compute the Borda scores for each alternative in Profile 1. Determine the social ranking. Who wins (has the highest Borda score)?

Profile 2: Same as Profile 1 except the alternative \(d\) is removed from the ballots (voters rank only \(\{a, b, c\}\)). - Voter 1: \(a \succ b \succ c\) (scores: \(a=2, b=1, c=0\)) - Voter 2: \(b \succ a \succ c\) (scores: \(a=1, b=2, c=0\)) - Voter 3: \(b \succ c \succ a\) (scores: \(a=0, b=2, c=1\))

(b) Compute the Borda scores for \(\{a, b, c\}\) in Profile 2. Who wins? Compare to Profile 1.

(c) Show that the relative ranking of \(a\) vs. \(b\) differs between Profile 1 and Profile 2, even though no voter changed their ranking of \(a\) vs. \(b\). Conclude that the Borda count violates Arrow’s axiom IIA. Exhibit the specific violation.

(d) Verify that the Borda count satisfies axioms U, P, and ND (briefly). Confirm that it must therefore violate IIA, consistent with Arrow’s theorem.

(e) Consider a modified axiom: Independence of Non-Dominated Alternatives (INDA): the social ranking of \(x\) vs. \(y\) depends only on the rank positions of \(x\) and \(y\) in each voter’s ordering (not on absolute scores). Does the Borda count satisfy INDA? Is INDA weaker or stronger than IIA? Can Arrow’s theorem be avoided by replacing IIA with INDA?
