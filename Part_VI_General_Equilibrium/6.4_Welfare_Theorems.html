43 6.4 Welfare Theorems

MWG Reference: Ch. 16, §16.C–16.D, pp. 549–579

43.1 Context

The welfare theorems are the mathematical formalization of Adam Smith’s invisible hand. The First Welfare Theorem states that competitive equilibrium is efficient — no central planner can improve on the market outcome (in the Pareto sense). The Second Welfare Theorem states the converse: any efficient allocation can be achieved by the market, provided the government redistributes wealth appropriately via lump-sum transfers. Together, they provide the normative foundation of competitive market theory: markets should be trusted to allocate resources efficiently, and distributional concerns should be addressed through redistribution, not interference with prices.

The First Welfare Theorem is a remarkably robust result — it requires only local nonsatiation, not convexity. The Second Welfare Theorem requires convexity, and its limitations (informational requirements of lump-sum transfers, non-convexities in reality) motivate the second-best theory.

43.2 Definitions

NoteDefinition 6.4.1 — Pareto Optimality

A feasible allocation \((x, y) = ((x_i)_{i \in I}, (y_j)_{j \in J})\) satisfying \(\sum_i x_i = \bar{\omega} + \sum_j y_j\) is Pareto optimal (PO) if there is no other feasible allocation \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y})\) such that: - \(u_i(\tilde{x}_i) \geq u_i(x_i)\) for all \(i \in I\) - \(u_j(\tilde{x}_j) > u_j(x_j)\) for at least one \(j \in I\)

An allocation is PO if and only if it solves: \[\max_{(x,y) \text{ feasible}} u_1(x_1) \text{ subject to } u_i(x_i) \geq \bar{u}_i\; \forall i \geq 2,\] for some vector of utility bounds \((\bar{u}_2, \ldots, \bar{u}_n)\).

43.3 Main Results

ImportantTheorem 6.4.1 — First Welfare Theorem (1WT)

If preferences \(\succeq_i\) satisfy local nonsatiation for all \(i\), then every Walrasian equilibrium allocation \((x^*, y^*, p^*)\) is Pareto optimal.

TipProof

Suppose for contradiction that \((x^*, y^*, p^*)\) is a Walrasian equilibrium but is not Pareto optimal. Then there exists a feasible allocation \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y})\) such that \(u_i(\tilde{x}_i) \geq u_i(x_i^*)\) for all \(i\), with strict inequality for some \(k\).

Step 1: Every consumer’s preferred bundle costs at least as much as their equilibrium bundle.

Since \(x_i^* \in x_i(p^*, w_i^*)\) (utility maximization), any bundle \(\tilde{x}_i\) preferred to \(x_i^*\) cannot be affordable at \((p^*, w_i^*)\): \[u_i(\tilde{x}_i) > u_i(x_i^*) \implies p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i > w_i^* = p^* \cdot x_i^*. \tag{1}\] (If \(\tilde{x}_i\) were affordable and preferred, it would have been chosen over \(x_i^*\).)

Step 2: Any bundle as good as \(x_i^*\) costs at least as much (for local nonsatiation).

For the consumer \(i\) with \(u_i(\tilde{x}_i) \geq u_i(x_i^*)\) but \(u_i(\tilde{x}_i) = u_i(x_i^*)\) (indifference), we need to show \(p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i \geq p^* \cdot x_i^*\). Suppose not: \(p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i < p^* \cdot x_i^* = w_i^*\). By local nonsatiation, there exists \(\tilde{x}_i'\) arbitrarily close to \(\tilde{x}_i\) with \(u_i(\tilde{x}_i') > u_i(\tilde{x}_i) = u_i(x_i^*)\) and \(p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i' \leq w_i^*\) (by continuity of the budget set). But this means \(\tilde{x}_i'\) is affordable and preferred to \(x_i^*\), contradicting the optimality of \(x_i^*\). Therefore: \[u_i(\tilde{x}_i) \geq u_i(x_i^*) \implies p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i \geq p^* \cdot x_i^*. \tag{2}\]

Step 3: Aggregate and derive a contradiction.

Summing (2) over all \(i\) with \(u_i(\tilde{x}_i) \geq u_i(x_i^*)\), and using (1) for the consumer \(k\) with strict preference: \[\sum_{i \in I} p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i > \sum_{i \in I} p^* \cdot x_i^* = \sum_{i \in I} w_i^* = p^* \cdot \bar{\omega} + \sum_{j \in J} \pi_j(p^*).\]

Since \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y})\) is feasible: \(\sum_i \tilde{x}_i = \bar{\omega} + \sum_j \tilde{y}_j\). Therefore: \[p^* \cdot \left(\bar{\omega} + \sum_j \tilde{y}_j\right) > p^* \cdot \bar{\omega} + \sum_j \pi_j(p^*)\] \[\sum_j p^* \cdot \tilde{y}_j > \sum_j \pi_j(p^*) = \sum_j \max_{y \in Y_j} p^* \cdot y.\]

But \(\tilde{y}_j \in Y_j\) by feasibility, so \(p^* \cdot \tilde{y}_j \leq \pi_j(p^*)\) for each \(j\). Summing: \(\sum_j p^* \cdot \tilde{y}_j \leq \sum_j \pi_j(p^*)\) — a contradiction. \(\blacksquare\)

CautionRemark

The proof uses only: - Individual optimization (demand and profit maximization) - Local nonsatiation (to establish that preferred bundles are more expensive) - Feasibility of the alternative allocation - No convexity is required.

ImportantTheorem 6.4.2 — Second Welfare Theorem (2WT)

Let \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y})\) be a Pareto optimal allocation. Under the following assumptions: - Consumer preferences \(\succeq_i\) are convex and continuous for all \(i\) - Production sets \(Y_j\) are convex for all \(j\) - \(\tilde{x}_i \gg 0\) for all \(i\) (interior optimal consumption)

There exists a price vector \(p^* \in \mathbb{R}^L_{++}\) and wealth levels \(w_i^*\) with \(\sum_i w_i^* = p^* \cdot \bar{\omega} + \sum_j \pi_j(p^*)\) such that \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y}, p^*)\) is a Walrasian equilibrium when consumers have wealth \(w_i^*\).

TipProof

The proof uses the separating hyperplane theorem to find the equilibrium price vector.

Step 1: Define the “better-than” sets.

For each consumer \(i\), define: \[\hat{P}_i = \{x_i \in \mathbb{R}^L_+ : u_i(x_i) \geq u_i(\tilde{x}_i)\}\] (the set of bundles at least as good as \(\tilde{x}_i\)).

By convexity of preferences, \(\hat{P}_i\) is a convex set. By continuity of \(u_i\), \(\hat{P}_i\) is closed. Since \(\tilde{x}_i \in \hat{P}_i\), it is nonempty.

Define: \[\hat{P} = \sum_{i \in I} \hat{P}_i = \left\{\sum_i x_i : x_i \in \hat{P}_i\right\}\] (the Minkowski sum of the individual better-than sets). \(\hat{P}\) is convex (sum of convex sets).

Step 2: Define the feasible set and apply the separating hyperplane theorem.

Define the aggregate feasible production set: \[Y = \bar{\omega} + \sum_{j \in J} Y_j = \left\{\bar{\omega} + \sum_j y_j : y_j \in Y_j\right\}.\] \(Y\) is convex (sum of convex sets).

Key claim: \(\hat{P} \cap \text{int}(Y) = \emptyset\).

Proof of claim: Suppose \(z \in \hat{P} \cap \text{int}(Y)\). Then \(z = \sum_i x_i'\) with \(u_i(x_i') \geq u_i(\tilde{x}_i)\) and \(z = \bar{\omega} + \sum_j y_j'\) with \(y_j' \in Y_j\), and \(z\) is in the interior of \(Y\). This means there exists \(\tilde{z} < z\) (componentwise) with \(\tilde{z} \in Y\) as well. But then the allocation with \(\sum_i x_i' = z > \tilde{z}\)… We need to be more careful.

Since \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y})\) is Pareto optimal, there is no feasible allocation improving all consumers. If \(z \in \hat{P}\), then \(z = \sum_i x_i'\) with each \(x_i'\) weakly preferred to \(\tilde{x}_i\). If such a \(z\) were feasible (in \(Y = \bar\omega + \sum_j Y_j\)), then \((x', y')\) would be a feasible allocation weakly preferred by all, with feasibility following from \(\sum_i x_i' = \bar\omega + \sum_j y_j'\). Pareto optimality of \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y})\) and the local nonsatiation would imply this is an improvement only if some consumer is strictly better off — but the definition of \(\hat{P}\) allows weak preference. For strict Pareto optimality to be contradicted, we need strict preference somewhere.

Using local nonsatiation more carefully: if \(z \in \hat{P}\) and \(z \in \text{int}(Y)\), there is a feasible \(z' = z - \epsilon \mathbf{1}\) for small \(\epsilon > 0\), still feasible. This allows us to redistribute slightly and still allocate \(\sum_i x_i' = z\), which is more than the feasible \(z'\), not less. The argument needs the interior condition to allow free disposal.

More precisely: if \(\sum_i \tilde{x}_i \in \text{int}(\bar{\omega} + \sum_j Y_j)\), then there exists a direction in which total resources could be reduced while remaining feasible, contradicting PO (we could reallocate the saved resources to strictly improve someone). Therefore \(\sum_i \tilde{x}_i\) is on the boundary of the feasible set, and \(\hat{P} \cap \text{int}(Y) = \emptyset\).

Step 3: Apply the separating hyperplane theorem.

Since \(\hat{P}\) and \(\text{int}(Y)\) are disjoint convex sets (with \(\hat{P}\) nonempty and \(\text{int}(Y)\) nonempty), by the separating hyperplane theorem there exists \(p^* \in \mathbb{R}^L \setminus \{0\}\) and \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\) such that: \[p^* \cdot z \geq \alpha \quad \forall z \in \hat{P}, \qquad p^* \cdot z \leq \alpha \quad \forall z \in Y.\]

Step 4: Show \(p^*\) is the equilibrium price vector.

  • Profit maximization: For each firm \(j\), since \(y_j \in Y_j \subset Y\) (adjusting the definition appropriately), \(p^* \cdot y_j \leq \alpha - p^* \cdot \bar{\omega}\), and equality is achieved at \(\tilde{y}_j\) (since \(\bar{\omega} + \tilde{y} \in Y\) and \(\sum_i \tilde{x}_i \in \hat{P}\) with \(p^* \cdot \sum_i \tilde{x}_i \geq \alpha \geq p^* \cdot (\bar{\omega} + \sum_j \tilde{y}_j)\), and by feasibility of \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y})\) these are equal). Therefore \(\tilde{y}_j \in \arg\max_{y \in Y_j} p^* \cdot y\) for each \(j\).

  • Utility maximization: From the separation, \(p^* \cdot x_i \geq p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i\) for any \(x_i\) weakly preferred to \(\tilde{x}_i\) (any \(x_i \in \hat{P}_i\) contributes at least \(p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i\) to the sum \(p^* \cdot z\)). Therefore \(\tilde{x}_i\) minimizes expenditure over the upper contour set, which is equivalent to utility maximization (expenditure minimization and utility maximization are dual by convexity of preferences). With wealth \(w_i^* = p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i\), consumer \(i\) optimally chooses \(\tilde{x}_i\).

  • Market clearing: Follows by construction: \(\sum_i \tilde{x}_i = \bar{\omega} + \sum_j \tilde{y}_j\).

Therefore \((\tilde{x}, \tilde{y}, p^*)\) is a Walrasian equilibrium with wealth redistribution \(w_i^* = p^* \cdot \tilde{x}_i\). \(\blacksquare\)

43.4 Limitations of the Second Welfare Theorem

Convexity. The Second WT requires convex preferences and production sets. With non-convexities (increasing returns, indivisibilities), a Pareto optimum cannot generally be decentralized as a competitive equilibrium. This is the fundamental market failure of natural monopolies and economies of scale.

Lump-sum transfers. Implementing the 2WT requires lump-sum taxes and subsidies: wealth transfers that do not distort relative prices. In practice, lump-sum transfers are informationally demanding (the government must know individual endowments or productivities) and politically constrained. The second-best theory (Ramsey, Diamond-Mirrlees) studies optimal taxation when lump-sum transfers are unavailable.

Distributional concerns. Both welfare theorems are silent on distribution: PO allocations can be extremely unequal. The 2WT says any PO allocation (including one giving all resources to one person) is a WE under some redistribution. The choice among Pareto-optimal allocations requires a social welfare function (see Part VIII).

43.5 Welfare Theorems with Externalities and Public Goods

When utilities or production functions depend directly on others’ actions, the proof of the 1WT fails. Specifically, Step 1 fails: a bundle preferred by consumer \(i\) may cost less at equilibrium prices, because \(i\) benefits from a positive externality generated by \(j\)’s purchase.

The 1WT holds in the presence of externalities only if: - Pigouvian taxes correct the wedge between private and social marginal costs (see 6.5 Externalities and Public Goods) - Property rights are well-defined and bargaining is costless (Coase theorem)

Public goods: With nonrival goods, the competitive market cannot internalize the social value. The 1WT fails because a consumer’s private willingness to pay understates the social benefit (which is the sum of all consumers’ valuations). Lindahl pricing restores efficiency conceptually but is not incentive-compatible.
