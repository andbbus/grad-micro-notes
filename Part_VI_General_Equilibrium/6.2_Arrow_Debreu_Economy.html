41 6.2 The Arrow-Debreu Economy

MWG Reference: Ch. 15, §15.D–15.E, pp. 546–570; Ch. 19, §19.B, pp. 688–703

41.1 Context

The Arrow-Debreu economy (Arrow-Debreu 1954, McKenzie 1954) extends the pure exchange model to include production. Firms possess production sets and profit-maximize; their profits are distributed to consumers as dividend income. This model is not only more general than exchange economies — it provides the complete competitive equilibrium framework that is the benchmark for all of modern microeconomics and macroeconomics. Arrow and Debreu’s further insight — that goods indexed by state of the world are formally identical to ordinary goods — gives the model its extraordinary scope: a single Arrow-Debreu equilibrium in the extended commodity space captures not only multi-market trade but also complete-market financial allocation.

41.2 Definitions

NoteDefinition 6.2.1 — Arrow-Debreu Economy

An Arrow-Debreu economy is \(\mathcal{E} = \big((u_i, \omega_i, (\theta_{ij})_{j \in J})_{i \in I},\, (Y_j)_{j \in J}\big)\) where: - \(I = \{1, \ldots, n\}\) consumers, \(J = \{1, \ldots, m\}\) firms, \(L\) goods - \(u_i: \mathbb{R}^L_+ \to \mathbb{R}\) is consumer \(i\)’s utility function; \(\omega_i \in \mathbb{R}^L_+\) is their endowment - \(\theta_{ij} \geq 0\) with \(\sum_{i \in I} \theta_{ij} = 1\) is consumer \(i\)’s ownership share of firm \(j\) - \(Y_j \subseteq \mathbb{R}^L\) is firm \(j\)’s production set: the set of technologically feasible input-output vectors \(y_j\) (by convention, inputs are negative, outputs positive)

NoteDefinition 6.2.2 — Profit-Maximizing Firm

At price vector \(p \in \mathbb{R}^L_{++}\), firm \(j\)’s profit-maximization problem is: \[\pi_j(p) = \max_{y_j \in Y_j} p \cdot y_j,\] with supply correspondence \(y_j^*(p) = \arg\max_{y_j \in Y_j} p \cdot y_j\).

NoteDefinition 6.2.3 — Consumer Wealth in Arrow-Debreu

Consumer \(i\)’s wealth at prices \(p\) is: \[w_i(p) = p \cdot \omega_i + \sum_{j \in J} \theta_{ij} \pi_j(p).\] Wealth comprises both the market value of the endowment and the dividend income from profit shares.

NoteDefinition 6.2.4 — Walrasian Equilibrium (Arrow-Debreu Economy)

A Walrasian equilibrium is a triple \((x^*, y^*, p^*)\) where: 1. \(x_i^* \in x_i(p^*, w_i(p^*))\) for each consumer \(i\) (utility maximization) 2. \(y_j^* \in y_j^*(p^*)\) for each firm \(j\) (profit maximization) 3. \(\sum_{i \in I} x_i^* = \sum_{i \in I} \omega_i + \sum_{j \in J} y_j^*\) (market clearing)

NoteDefinition 6.2.5 — Aggregate Excess Demand (with Production)

\[z(p) = \sum_{i \in I} x_i(p, w_i(p)) - \sum_{j \in J} y_j^*(p) - \bar{\omega},\] where \(\bar{\omega} = \sum_i \omega_i\). Equilibrium requires \(z(p^*) = 0\).

41.3 Main Results

ImportantTheorem 6.2.1 — Walras’ Law in the Arrow-Debreu Economy

Under local nonsatiation of consumer preferences, \(p \cdot z(p) = 0\) for all \(p \gg 0\).

TipProof

By local nonsatiation, each consumer exhausts their budget: \[p \cdot x_i(p, w_i(p)) = w_i(p) = p \cdot \omega_i + \sum_j \theta_{ij}\pi_j(p) \quad \forall i.\] Summing over consumers: \[\sum_i p \cdot x_i = \sum_i p \cdot \omega_i + \sum_i \sum_j \theta_{ij}\pi_j(p) = p \cdot \bar{\omega} + \sum_j \pi_j(p) \sum_i \theta_{ij}.\] Since \(\sum_i \theta_{ij} = 1\) for each \(j\): \[\sum_i p \cdot x_i = p \cdot \bar{\omega} + \sum_j \pi_j(p) = p \cdot \bar{\omega} + \sum_j p \cdot y_j^*(p).\] Therefore: \[p \cdot z(p) = p \cdot \left(\sum_i x_i - \sum_j y_j^* - \bar{\omega}\right) = \left(p \cdot \bar{\omega} + \sum_j p \cdot y_j^*\right) - \sum_j p \cdot y_j^* - p \cdot \bar{\omega} = 0. \quad \blacksquare\]

ImportantTheorem 6.2.2 — Homogeneity of Aggregate Excess Demand

The aggregate excess demand \(z(p)\) is homogeneous of degree zero in \(p\).

TipProof

Both \(x_i(p, w_i(p))\) and \(y_j^*(p)\) are homogeneous of degree zero in \(p\): - Consumer demand: Walrasian demand is homogeneous of degree zero in \((p, w)\). Since \(w_i(\lambda p) = \lambda p \cdot \omega_i + \sum_j \theta_{ij} \pi_j(\lambda p)\). Profit \(\pi_j(\lambda p) = \max_{y \in Y_j} \lambda p \cdot y = \lambda \pi_j(p)\) (homogeneous of degree one in \(p\)). Therefore \(w_i(\lambda p) = \lambda w_i(p)\). Hence \(x_i(\lambda p, \lambda w_i(p)) = x_i(p, w_i(p))\). - Firm supply: \(y_j^*(\lambda p) = \arg\max_{y \in Y_j} \lambda p \cdot y = \arg\max_{y \in Y_j} p \cdot y = y_j^*(p)\).

Therefore \(z(\lambda p) = z(p)\). \(\blacksquare\)

41.4 Arrow-Debreu Contingent Commodities

The most profound generalization of the Arrow-Debreu framework is its extension to uncertainty.

NoteDefinition 6.2.6 — Arrow-Debreu Contingent Commodity

Let \(\Omega = \{1, \ldots, S\}\) be a finite set of states of the world. An Arrow-Debreu contingent commodity \(\ell_s\) is a unit of good \(\ell\) delivered if and only if state \(s\) occurs. The extended commodity space is \(\mathbb{R}^{L \times S}\), with \(LS\) commodities.

A complete markets equilibrium (Arrow-Debreu equilibrium under uncertainty) is a Walrasian equilibrium in this \(LS\)-commodity space.

CautionRemark

Defining goods by \((commodity, state)\) pairs formally reduces GE under uncertainty to the standard GE framework. Consumers maximize expected utility over state-contingent consumption bundles; firms maximize expected profits. The existence theorem and welfare theorems apply without modification.

A price \(p_{\ell s}\) for contingent commodity \(\ell_s\) is an Arrow-Debreu price: it is the current price of a commitment to deliver one unit of good \(\ell\) in state \(s\). Under complete markets, all risk is traded away in equilibrium.

NoteDefinition 6.2.7 — Radner Equilibrium

A Radner equilibrium (Radner 1972) is an equilibrium in a sequential market structure where: - At \(t = 0\) (before the state is realized), there are markets for assets (financial instruments) - At \(t = 1\) (after the state is realized), there are spot markets for goods

Agents trade assets at \(t = 0\) to transfer wealth across states; they trade goods at \(t = 1\) given their realized wealth.

ImportantTheorem 6.2.3 — Equivalence of Radner and Arrow-Debreu Equilibria

If there are complete asset markets (the asset payoff matrix has rank equal to the number of states \(S\)), then the set of Radner equilibrium allocations coincides with the set of Arrow-Debreu equilibrium allocations.

TipProof

Proof sketch.

From Arrow-Debreu to Radner: Given an Arrow-Debreu equilibrium with contingent prices \(p_{\ell s}\), define asset portfolios that replicate the contingent commodity purchases. Each consumer’s Arrow-Debreu consumption plan can be decomposed into a financial portfolio (purchased at \(t = 0\)) and spot market purchases (at \(t = 1\)). With complete markets (rank \(S\) asset payoff matrix), any contingent wealth transfer is achievable.

From Radner to Arrow-Debreu: A Radner equilibrium with spot prices \(q_{\ell s}\) (in state \(s\)) and asset prices \(r\) satisfies: each agent maximizes utility subject to financial feasibility at \(t = 0\) and budget constraints at \(t = 1\) for each state. Define Arrow-Debreu prices \(p_{\ell s} = q_{\ell s} \cdot \phi_s\) where \(\phi_s\) is the state price (the \(t = 0\) price of one unit of account in state \(s\), derived from asset prices). Under complete markets, state prices are uniquely determined. The Radner optimal consumption plan satisfies the Arrow-Debreu budget constraint at prices \(\{p_{\ell s}\}\), and market clearing at each state maps to Arrow-Debreu market clearing. \(\blacksquare\)

CautionRemark

The Arrow-Debreu/Radner equivalence theorem is the foundation of financial economics. It implies that in complete markets, the specific trading arrangement (all contingent claims at date 0 vs. sequential trading with assets) is irrelevant for allocations. Market incompleteness — when the rank of the asset payoff matrix is less than \(S\) — is the fundamental source of financial market failures: agents cannot perfectly insure against all risks, and equilibrium allocations are generally constrained inefficient.
