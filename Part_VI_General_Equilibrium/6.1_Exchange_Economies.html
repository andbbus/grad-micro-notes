39  6.1 Exchange Economies

40 6.1 Exchange Economies

MWG Reference: Ch. 15, §15.A–15.C, pp. 505–545

40.1 Context

The pure exchange economy is the simplest general equilibrium model: there is no production, only redistribution of a fixed aggregate endowment through trade. Despite its simplicity, the exchange economy contains all the essential conceptual apparatus: competitive equilibrium, excess demand, Walras’ Law, price normalization, and the geometric intuition of the Edgeworth box. These are the foundations on which the Arrow-Debreu production economy and the welfare theorems are built.

40.2 Definitions

NoteDefinition 6.1.1 — Pure Exchange Economy

A pure exchange economy \(\mathcal{E}\) consists of: - \(I = \{1, \ldots, n\}\) consumers and \(L\) goods - Consumer \(i\)’s endowment \(\omega_i \in \mathbb{R}^L_+\): the bundle they bring to the market - Consumer \(i\)’s preference relation \(\succeq_i\) (or utility function \(u_i: \mathbb{R}^L_+ \to \mathbb{R}\)) over consumption bundles - Total (aggregate) endowment: \(\bar{\omega} = \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_i \in \mathbb{R}^L_{++}\)

NoteDefinition 6.1.2 — Walrasian (Competitive) Equilibrium

A Walrasian equilibrium of \(\mathcal{E}\) is a pair \((x^*, p^*)\) where \(x^* = (x_1^*, \ldots, x_n^*)\) and \(p^* \in \mathbb{R}^L_{++}\) such that: 1. Individual optimality: For each \(i\), \(x_i^* \in x_i(p^*, p^* \cdot \omega_i)\), where \(x_i(p, w)\) is consumer \(i\)’s Walrasian demand correspondence at price \(p\) and wealth \(w\) 2. Market clearing: \(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^* = \bar{\omega}\) (the allocation is feasible and exhausts the endowment)

NoteDefinition 6.1.3 — Excess Demand

The excess demand function (or correspondence) is \[z(p) = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i(p, p \cdot \omega_i) - \bar{\omega}.\] Equilibrium requires \(z(p^*) = 0_{\mathbb{R}^L}\) (every market clears).

NoteDefinition 6.1.4 — Edgeworth Box

In a two-consumer, two-good economy with total endowment \(\bar{\omega} = (\bar{\omega}_1, \bar{\omega}_2)\), the Edgeworth box is the rectangle of feasible allocations: \[\mathcal{F} = \{(x_1, x_2) \in \mathbb{R}^4_+ : x_1 + x_2 = \bar{\omega}\}.\] Consumer 1’s origin is the southwest corner; consumer 2’s origin is the northeast corner. Any point \((x_1, x_2) \in \mathcal{F}\) represents a feasible allocation.

NoteDefinition 6.1.5 — Pareto Set and Contract Curve

An allocation \((x_1, x_2) \in \mathcal{F}\) is Pareto optimal if there is no other feasible \((x_1', x_2')\) such that \(u_i(x_i') \geq u_i(x_i)\) for all \(i\) and \(u_j(x_j') > u_j(x_j)\) for some \(j\).

The contract curve (or Pareto set) is the set of all Pareto optimal allocations in the Edgeworth box. In the interior, it consists of points where the consumers’ indifference curves are tangent, i.e., \(MRS_1 = MRS_2\).

40.3 Main Results

ImportantTheorem 6.1.1 — Walras’ Law

If each consumer’s preference relation \(\succeq_i\) satisfies local nonsatiation, then the excess demand function satisfies: \[p \cdot z(p) = 0 \quad \text{for all } p \in \mathbb{R}^L_{++}.\]

TipProof

By local nonsatiation, each consumer exhausts their budget at any optimal choice: \[p \cdot x_i^*(p, p \cdot \omega_i) = p \cdot \omega_i \quad \forall i.\] (If the budget constraint were not binding, there would exist a preferred bundle arbitrarily close to \(x_i^*\), contradicting optimality.)

Summing over all consumers: \[\sum_{i=1}^n p \cdot x_i(p, p \cdot \omega_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n p \cdot \omega_i.\] Rearranging: \[p \cdot \left(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i(p, p \cdot \omega_i) - \sum_{i=1}^n \omega_i\right) = 0.\] By Definition 6.1.3, this is \(p \cdot z(p) = 0\). \(\blacksquare\)

CautionRemark

Walras’ Law has a crucial implication for market clearing. If there are \(L\) goods and \(L-1\) markets clear (i.e., \(z_\ell(p^*) = 0\) for \(\ell = 1, \ldots, L-1\)), then: \[p^* \cdot z(p^*) = 0 \Rightarrow \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell^* z_\ell(p^*) = 0 \Rightarrow p_L^* z_L(p^*) = 0.\] Since \(p_L^* > 0\) in equilibrium, it follows that \(z_L(p^*) = 0\). The \(L\)-th market clears automatically. This reduces the number of independent equilibrium conditions to \(L-1\).

ImportantTheorem 6.1.2 — Properties of Excess Demand

Under standard assumptions (continuity and local nonsatiation of preferences, positive endowments), the excess demand function \(z: \mathbb{R}^L_{++} \to \mathbb{R}^L\) satisfies: 1. Walras’ Law: \(p \cdot z(p) = 0\) for all \(p \gg 0\) 2. Homogeneity of degree zero: \(z(\lambda p) = z(p)\) for all \(\lambda > 0\) 3. Continuity: \(z\) is continuous on \(\mathbb{R}^L_{++}\) 4. Boundary condition: As \(p_\ell \to 0\), \(z_\ell(p) \to +\infty\) (demand for a free good is unbounded)

TipProof

Homogeneity: Each individual demand \(x_i(p, p \cdot \omega_i)\) is homogeneous of degree zero in \((p, w)\) since \(w_i = p \cdot \omega_i\) scales proportionally with \(p\). Therefore \(x_i(\lambda p, \lambda p \cdot \omega_i) = x_i(p, p \cdot \omega_i)\), and so \(z(\lambda p) = z(p)\).

Continuity: Under standard preference assumptions (continuous, strictly convex), Walrasian demand \(x_i(p, w)\) is continuous. Continuity of \(z\) follows from continuity of the sum.

Boundary condition: As \(p_\ell \to 0\), good \(\ell\) becomes free. By local nonsatiation, each consumer wants more of every good (or at worst, is indifferent). With free good \(\ell\), the budget set becomes effectively unconstrained in dimension \(\ell\), and demand for \(\ell\) grows without bound. Formally, the Walrasian demand \(x_{i\ell}(p, w) \to +\infty\) as \(p_\ell \to 0^+\) for any fixed \(w\) (since the wealth buys infinite units of good \(\ell\)). Therefore \(z_\ell(p) \to +\infty\). \(\blacksquare\)

ImportantTheorem 6.1.3 — Price Normalization

Due to homogeneity of degree zero, it is without loss of generality to restrict attention to prices in the unit simplex: \[\Delta^{L-1} = \left\{p \in \mathbb{R}^L_+ : \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell = 1\right\}.\] Any equilibrium price vector \(p^* \in \mathbb{R}^L_{++}\) corresponds to a normalized price \(p^*/\|p^*\|_1 \in \Delta^{L-1}\) that is also an equilibrium (since excess demand is unchanged).

40.4 The Edgeworth Box: Geometric Intuition

NoteExample 6.1.1 — Walrasian Equilibrium in the Edgeworth Box

Consider 2 consumers, 2 goods: \(u_1(x_{11}, x_{12}) = x_{11}^\alpha x_{12}^{1-\alpha}\), \(u_2(x_{21}, x_{22}) = x_{21}^\beta x_{22}^{1-\beta}\). Endowments: \(\omega_1 = (1, 0)\), \(\omega_2 = (0, 1)\).

Walrasian demand: With Cobb-Douglas preferences, consumer \(i\)’s demand for good \(\ell\) is a fixed fraction of wealth: \[x_{11}(p, w_1) = \frac{\alpha w_1}{p_1} = \frac{\alpha p_1}{p_1} = \alpha, \quad x_{12}(p, w_1) = \frac{(1-\alpha)p_1}{p_2}.\] \[x_{21}(p, w_2) = \frac{\beta p_2}{p_1}, \quad x_{22}(p, w_2) = \frac{(1-\beta)p_2}{p_2} = 1-\beta.\]

Market clearing for good 1: \(x_{11} + x_{21} = 1\): \[\alpha + \frac{\beta p_2}{p_1} = 1 \Rightarrow \frac{p_2}{p_1} = \frac{1-\alpha}{\beta}.\]

Equilibrium price ratio: \(p_1/p_2 = \beta/(1-\alpha)\).

Equilibrium allocation: \(x_1^* = (\alpha, (1-\alpha)\beta/\beta) = (\alpha, 1-\alpha)\) and \(x_2^* = (1-\alpha, \beta)\)… adjusting: \(x_{12}^* = (1-\alpha)p_1/p_2 = (1-\alpha) \cdot \beta/(1-\alpha) = \beta\), so \(x_1^* = (\alpha, \beta)\) and… Let us recompute. With \(p_2/p_1 = (1-\alpha)/\beta\): - \(w_1 = p_1 \cdot 1 = p_1\); \(x_{11}^* = \alpha\), \(x_{12}^* = (1-\alpha)p_1/p_2 = (1-\alpha)\beta/(1-\alpha) = \beta\). - \(w_2 = p_2 \cdot 1 = p_2\); \(x_{21}^* = \beta p_2/p_1 = \beta(1-\alpha)/\beta = 1-\alpha\), \(x_{22}^* = 1-\beta\).

Equilibrium allocation: \(x_1^* = (\alpha, \beta)\), \(x_2^* = (1-\alpha, 1-\beta)\). Market clearing: \(\alpha + (1-\alpha) = 1\) and \(\beta + (1-\beta) = 1\). Confirmed.

40.5 Core and Walrasian Equilibrium

The core of the exchange economy is the set of feasible allocations that cannot be blocked by any coalition: allocation \(x\) is in the core if no subset \(S \subseteq I\) can redistribute their endowments \(\sum_{i \in S}\omega_i\) to make all members strictly better off.

Every Walrasian equilibrium allocation is in the core (this follows from the First Welfare Theorem plus the ability of blocking coalitions to use only their own endowments). The core convergence theorem (Edgeworth, formalized by Debreu-Scarf 1963) states that as the economy is replicated infinitely many times, the core shrinks to the set of Walrasian allocations.
